\title{Beyond One-Size-Fits-All: An Adaptive AI Interview System with Lightweight Calibration, Multi-Priority Selection, and Hybrid LLM Evaluation}

\author{Anonymous Authors}

\begin{abstract}
\noindent AI-powered technical interviews promise scalability and consistency, yet most systems treat candidates uniformly---fixed difficulty, generic questions, and brittle evaluation---undermining both assessment validity and candidate experience. We identify three design gaps. First, \emph{calibration}: systems fail to adapt difficulty to candidate ability in real time. Second, \emph{selection}: questions ignore knowledge gaps and candidate backgrounds. Third, \emph{evaluation}: reliability and nuance are traded off, and LLM-only systems lack graceful degradation. We present an adaptive interview system that addresses these gaps through four innovations. We use a \emph{lightweight sliding-window} calibration that converges within 4--5 questions without IRT calibration. We employ a \emph{multi-priority} selection strategy (gap targeting, resume personalization, \emph{LLM next-direction feedback}, coverage) with optional IRT-inspired and UCB-based enhancements. We combine rule-based scoring with validated LLM augmentation and automatic fallback in a \emph{hybrid evaluation}. We add an \emph{LLM-enhanced report generator} that produces personalized summaries, strengths/weaknesses, learning plans, speech recommendations, and question rationales, all with rule-based fallback. Empirical evaluation demonstrates improved calibration, gap-targeting, coverage, and agreement with human evaluators, with high uptime through automatic fallback. We contribute design principles for human-centered adaptive assessment, reproducible algorithms, and implications for fairness and candidate experience in AI-assisted hiring.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Existing Problems in Technical Interview Assessment}

\noindent Technical interviews are a cornerstone of hiring in software engineering, yet they remain a source of stress and inequity for candidates \cite{kim2024interview}. AI-powered systems promise to scale assessment consistently, but most simply replicate existing problems at scale. Poorly calibrated assessments---questions that are too easy or too hard---waste time, reduce validity, and harm candidate experience. From a human-centered design perspective, these failures matter because they affect both \emph{assessment validity} (do we measure what we intend?) and \emph{candidate experience} (do candidates feel fairly and accurately assessed?). We identify three design gaps that limit current systems.

\noindent \textbf{Problem 1: Inconsistent difficulty calibration.} Traditional interviews employ fixed difficulty levels that may be too challenging for some candidates while insufficiently challenging for others, leading to inaccurate ability estimation and poor candidate experience \cite{van2006computerized}. Interviewers struggle to dynamically adjust question difficulty based on real-time performance, often relying on intuition rather than systematic algorithms. This results in interviews that fail to efficiently converge on accurate ability estimates, wasting time on questions that are either too easy or too difficult. In AI-powered systems, the problem persists: most platforms use fixed templates or simple rule-based adjustments that lack rapid calibration within the limited question counts typical of technical interviews \cite{kim2024interview}.

\noindent \textbf{Problem 2: Ineffective question selection.} Current approaches either employ random selection, which fails to target knowledge gaps, or use rigid templates that ignore candidate backgrounds and performance history \cite{chen2022personalization}. The challenge lies in simultaneously addressing identified weaknesses through targeted questions while ensuring comprehensive coverage across knowledge domains. Additionally, question selection must consider candidate backgrounds from resumes to personalize the assessment experience, yet existing systems rarely integrate resume analysis into selection algorithms \cite{li2022resume}. Systems that prioritize one objective (e.g., ability matching) often sacrifice coverage or personalization \cite{vanlehn2011relative}.

\noindent \textbf{Problem 3: Reliability--nuance trade-off in evaluation.} Rule-based evaluation systems provide consistent, reliable scoring but lack the nuanced understanding required for complex technical responses \cite{patel2023reliability,burrows2015eraser}. Conversely, purely LLM-based evaluation offers sophisticated analysis but introduces variability and dependency on external services \cite{chang2023survey,zhang2023evaluation}. Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) such as GPT-4 \cite{achiam2023gpt4} and instruction-following models \cite{ouyang2022chatgpt} have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in understanding and evaluating complex responses, yet their reliability in high-stakes assessment contexts remains a concern \cite{kumar2022fairness}. The challenge is developing a framework that combines the reliability of rule-based approaches with the sophistication of LLM analysis while maintaining system availability when external services fail.

\subsection{Existing Methods and Their Limitations}

\noindent Several research strands have attempted to address these problems, yet each falls short for technical interview contexts.

\noindent \textbf{Difficulty calibration.} Computerized adaptive testing (CAT) has addressed difficulty calibration through Item Response Theory (IRT) and ability estimation algorithms \cite{van2006computerized,chen2021adaptive}. These methods dynamically adjust test difficulty based on candidate responses and have achieved success in educational assessment \cite{hambleton1991fundamentals}. However, CAT approaches primarily target contexts with well-defined item banks and response models. Technical interviews present unique challenges: diverse question types, subjective evaluation criteria, and the need for rapid calibration within limited question counts. Existing CAT systems require extensive calibration data and typically need more questions than a practical interview allows to stabilize difficulty \cite{van2006computerized}.

\noindent \textbf{Question selection.} Intelligent tutoring systems employ adaptive algorithms for personalized learning and question selection \cite{vanlehn2011relative}, but focus on instruction rather than assessment. Question selection in these systems prioritizes learning objectives over comprehensive evaluation, making direct application to interviews inappropriate. Recent work on personalized question selection \cite{chen2022personalization} has shown promise in adaptive assessment contexts, but integration with candidate background information (e.g., resume parsing \cite{li2022resume}) remains limited. In interview settings, additional constraints---time, question budget, and high-stakes decision making---require explicitly defined protocols and robustness \cite{kumar2022fairness}.

\noindent \textbf{Evaluation.} Evaluation research has explored both rule-based approaches using text similarity and keyword matching \cite{burrows2015eraser,mohler2011evaluation} and LLM-based assessment. Modern language models have shown remarkable capabilities in understanding context and generating nuanced evaluations \cite{brown2020gpt3,raffel2020t5}, with recent instruction-tuned models demonstrating improved reliability \cite{ouyang2022chatgpt}. However, rule-based systems struggle with semantic understanding and context, while LLM systems face reliability and consistency challenges \cite{chang2023survey}. Hybrid approaches combining rule-based and learning-based methods have shown promise in various domains \cite{smith2021hybrid}, but remain underexplored in technical interview contexts where both reliability and nuanced analysis are critical, and graceful degradation when LLM services are unavailable is essential.

\subsection{Our Solution and Contributions}

\noindent We present an integrated adaptive interview system that addresses the three design gaps through innovations grounded in psychometrics (IRT-inspired ability estimation, Fisher information), bandit algorithms (UCB for exploration--exploitation), and human-centered design (resume-informed personalization, misconception-aware follow-ups, hybrid evaluation with fallback). Our approach is \emph{novel} in four ways. First, we adapt CAT principles to interview constraints without requiring pre-calibrated item banks. Second, we unify gap targeting, \emph{LLM next-direction feedback}, personalization, and coverage in a single multi-priority framework. Third, we demonstrate that hybrid rule+LLM evaluation can achieve both reliability and nuance with graceful degradation. Fourth, we extend the hybrid principle to report generation, producing personalized summaries and recommendations with rule-based fallback.

\noindent \textbf{Solution 1 (RQ1):} We propose a sliding-window weighted difficulty adjustment algorithm that dynamically calibrates to candidate ability levels in real time. Unlike traditional CAT approaches that require extensive calibration, our algorithm uses a small sliding window (default three questions) with weighted averaging that emphasizes recent performance while considering trends. This enables rapid convergence on appropriate difficulty levels within the constraints of interview contexts. We also support an optional target-score control strategy for scenarios prioritizing faster convergence.

\noindent \textbf{Solution 2 (RQ2):} We introduce a multi-priority question selection strategy that simultaneously targets knowledge gaps, considers candidate backgrounds, and ensures comprehensive coverage. The strategy employs three priority levels: missing knowledge chapters identified through previous evaluations (augmented by \emph{LLM next-direction hints} when the evaluator suggests follow-up topics), resume-based matching using automated resume parsing techniques \cite{li2022resume} and fuzzy string matching \cite{zhang2020fuzzy}, and weighted random or Thompson sampling from track-specific chapters. The system also supports a personalized mode integrating IRT-inspired ability estimation, Fisher information maximization, and UCB-based exploration for improved measurement efficiency.

\noindent \textbf{Solution 3 (RQ3):} We develop a hybrid evaluation framework that combines rule-based scoring with optional LLM enhancement \cite{smith2021hybrid}. The framework employs five-dimensional evaluation (correctness, depth, clarity, practicality, tradeoffs) through rule-based algorithms that ensure reliability and consistency. Optional LLM enhancement leverages instruction-following models \cite{ouyang2022chatgpt,achiam2023gpt4} and chain-of-thought reasoning \cite{wei2022chain} to provide nuanced analysis when available, with automatic fallback to rule-based evaluation ensuring system availability. This hybrid approach addresses the reliability--nuance trade-off while maintaining evaluation reliability standards \cite{patel2023reliability}.

\noindent \textbf{Research Questions.} This research addresses three fundamental questions:

\textbf{RQ1:} How can adaptive difficulty adjustment algorithms effectively calibrate to candidate ability levels in real-time technical interviews, achieving accurate ability estimation within limited question counts?

\textbf{RQ2:} How can intelligent question selection strategies balance knowledge gap targeting with comprehensive domain coverage while personalizing assessment based on candidate backgrounds?

\textbf{RQ3:} How can hybrid evaluation frameworks combining rule-based and LLM approaches provide both reliable baseline assessment and nuanced analysis while maintaining system availability?

\noindent \textbf{Contributions.} We make five contributions (C1--C5):
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{C1 (Technical)}: A lightweight difficulty calibration method that converges within 4--5 questions \emph{without} IRT calibration---a novel adaptation of CAT principles to interview constraints.
    \item \textbf{C2 (Technical)}: A multi-priority question selection framework that unifies gap targeting, \emph{LLM next-direction feedback} (evaluation-to-selection loop), resume personalization, and coverage, with optional IRT-inspired and UCB-based enhancements for measurement efficiency.
    \item \textbf{C3 (Technical)}: A hybrid evaluation pipeline combining rule-based reliability with validated LLM augmentation and automatic fallback, resolving the reliability--nuance trade-off while ensuring high uptime.
    \item \textbf{C4 (Technical)}: An LLM-enhanced report generator that produces personalized overall summaries, strengths/weaknesses descriptions, learning plans, speech improvement recommendations, and question rationales---all with rule-based fallback for availability.
    \item \textbf{C5 (Design)}: Design principles and empirical evidence for human-centered adaptive assessment, including implications for candidate experience, fairness, and graceful degradation in AI-assisted hiring.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Computerized Adaptive Testing}

\noindent Researchers have extensively studied Computerized Adaptive Testing (CAT) in educational assessment contexts \cite{van2006computerized}. The fundamental principle involves dynamically adjusting test difficulty based on candidate responses, grounded in Item Response Theory (IRT) \cite{hambleton1991fundamentals}. Recent advances have explored lightweight calibration methods and rapid convergence strategies \cite{chen2021adaptive}. ROAR-CAT \cite{roarcat2025} demonstrates the gold-standard CAT development pipeline: IRT parameter estimation on 1,960 students, followed by validation with 485 students, achieving 40\% efficiency gains (75 adaptive items matching the precision of 125 fixed items, $r = .89$). However, many CAT variants rely on pre-calibrated item parameters and response models that do not directly apply to technical interviews, where items are heterogeneous and scoring is multi-dimensional. Our work targets the interview setting by proposing a lightweight, reproducible sliding-window update rule that does not assume pre-calibrated IRT parameters, converging within 4--5 questions under a fixed question budget.

\subsection{Intelligent Tutoring Systems}

\noindent Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS) employ adaptive algorithms to personalize learning experiences based on student performance and knowledge gaps \cite{vanlehn2011relative}. While ITS primarily focus on educational delivery, they share similar adaptive principles with assessment systems. The key difference lies in our focus on evaluation rather than instruction, though we incorporate similar mechanisms for identifying knowledge gaps and adjusting content difficulty. However, ITS question selection prioritizes learning objectives over comprehensive evaluation, making direct application to interviews inappropriate. Our multi-priority strategy addresses RQ2 by balancing learning-oriented gap targeting with assessment-oriented coverage requirements.

\subsection{AI-Powered Interview Systems and HCI}

\noindent Prior work has explored AI support for interviews and assessment, including automated scoring and feedback pipelines \cite{kim2024interview}. Recent surveys highlight the growing adoption of AI-powered interview systems \cite{wang2023survey}. Conversate \cite{conversate2024} uses LLM-driven interactive simulation with adaptive follow-up questions and dialogic feedback; a 19-participant study found that adaptive follow-ups enhanced realism and two-way feedback promoted reflective learning. Virtual Interviewers \cite{virtual_interviewers2025} conducted a 20-participant formative study on multimodal mock technical interviews, reporting increased confidence via Wilcoxon signed-rank tests. A modular AI interviewer \cite{modular_interviewer2025} with local LLMs achieved high satisfaction (4.45/5) and engagement (4.33/5) scores. From an HCI perspective, candidate experience and perceived fairness are critical: poorly designed systems can induce anxiety, reduce performance, and perpetuate bias \cite{kumar2022fairness}. Multimodal approaches integrating text, audio, and video analysis have shown promise \cite{wang2022multimodal}, but reliability and fairness concerns remain. We therefore emphasize reproducible algorithm definitions, fairness constraints (same bank/budget/initialization across baselines), and reliability-oriented evaluation with human rubrics.

\subsection{Natural Language Processing for Assessment}

\noindent Text similarity and semantic analysis techniques have been applied to automated answer evaluation \cite{burrows2015eraser,mohler2011evaluation}. Pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT) \cite{devlin2018bert} enable stronger semantic representations, and recent large language models have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in understanding and evaluating complex responses \cite{brown2020gpt3,achiam2023gpt4}. Prompt engineering techniques, including chain-of-thought reasoning \cite{wei2022chain} and instruction following \cite{ouyang2022chatgpt}, have further improved LLM performance in evaluation tasks. However, their use in high-stakes scoring requires careful reliability protocols \cite{patel2023reliability,chang2023survey}. Comprehensive evaluation frameworks for LLMs highlight the importance of reliability, consistency, and human alignment in assessment contexts \cite{zhang2023evaluation}. A meta-analysis of 65 LLM-based automated scoring studies \cite{llm_aes_meta2025} reports agreement indices (QWK, Pearson $r$) mostly in the 0.30--0.80 range, with substantial variability across tasks and models. Targeted formative assessment studies \cite{llm_formative2025} show that context-enhanced few-shot chain-of-thought prompting substantially improves LLM--human agreement. An AI-driven mock interview study \cite{ai_mock_ijmlc2025} compared GPT-3.5 scoring against human evaluators using Cohen's $\kappa$, finding that LLMs approach human-level agreement on technical questions. Our hybrid evaluator combines a deterministic rule-based baseline with optional LLM augmentation and validation, not as a fully automated “oracle”.

\subsection{Adaptive Difficulty Adjustment}

\noindent Most existing interview platforms employ fixed difficulty levels or simple rule-based adjustments. Traditional CAT systems use IRT-based methods that require extensive calibration data and typically need more questions than a practical interview allows to stabilize difficulty. In our pilot protocol (Section~7), we observe that a lightweight sliding-window update can converge within a small number of questions. We report concrete values in Section~10 and provide data and script requirements in Reproducibility Notes (Section~7.6).

LLM-only systems often lack explicit difficulty calibration mechanisms, relying on implicit adjustments through question selection. While this can work, it lacks the systematic approach and measurable accuracy of our method. Our algorithm provides transparent, explainable difficulty adjustments with quantifiable performance metrics.

\subsection{Question Selection Strategies}

\noindent Fixed template systems lack personalization and cannot adapt to candidate performance or knowledge gaps. Simple CAT systems match questions to estimated ability but ignore knowledge gaps and candidate backgrounds. Our multi-priority strategy uniquely combines gap targeting, \emph{LLM next-direction feedback} (the evaluator's suggested follow-up topics are fuzzy-matched to chapters and fed into selection), resume-based personalization, and comprehensive coverage, achieving superior balance across competing objectives (quantitative results in Section~10).

Existing systems typically prioritize one objective: either coverage (fixed templates), ability matching (CAT), or personalization (some LLM systems). Our multi-priority framework dynamically balances these objectives, ensuring comprehensive assessment while maintaining personalization and gap targeting effectiveness.

\subsection{Evaluation Methodology}

\noindent Most existing systems employ either rule-based evaluation (reliable but limited nuance) or LLM-only evaluation (nuanced but unreliable). Our hybrid framework uniquely combines both approaches, achieving high agreement with human evaluators while maintaining high system availability through automatic fallback (quantitative results in Section~10). This represents a significant improvement over LLM-only systems that fail when API services are unavailable, and rule-based systems that lack nuanced analysis.

Traditional platforms rely on human evaluators, providing high quality but lacking scalability and consistency. Our hybrid approach provides consistent, scalable evaluation with human-level agreement in most cases, while maintaining system reliability through rule-based fallback.

\subsection{Personalization Capabilities}

\noindent Our system uniquely integrates resume parsing with intelligent question selection, enabling personalized interviews based on candidate backgrounds. While some LLM systems can process resumes, they lack the systematic integration with question selection that our multi-priority strategy provides. Fixed template and CAT systems typically ignore candidate backgrounds entirely.

The fuzzy matching approach we employ (70\% similarity threshold) enables robust matching even when chapter names and resume skills have slight variations, providing more reliable personalization than exact matching approaches used in some systems.

\subsection{System Availability and Reliability}

\noindent A critical differentiator is our system's ability to operate without LLM services. While LLM-only systems provide sophisticated analysis, they depend entirely on external API availability. Our hybrid framework maintains high uptime through rule-based fallback, ensuring continuous operation even when LLM services fail (see Section~10). This reliability is essential for production deployment where service interruptions are unacceptable.

The automatic fallback mechanism seamlessly transitions between LLM-enhanced and rule-based evaluation without user intervention, maintaining evaluation quality while ensuring system availability. This dual-mode operation is unique among existing systems.

\subsection{Multimodal Support}

\noindent Our system supports text, audio, and \emph{optional facial expression} inputs. Speech analysis provides communication metrics (fluency, nervousness, speech rate). Expression analysis provides facial-emotion-derived metrics (nervousness, confidence, engagement) through real-time video streaming. A live video preview appears in the corner during the interview, and the system samples frames in the background for analysis. While some platforms support audio input, few provide the combined speech and expression analysis that our system offers. This multimodal capability enables more holistic candidate assessment beyond technical knowledge. We treat both modalities as auxiliary signals and report them descriptively in the assessment report.

\subsection{Summary of Advantages}

\noindent Our system offers several key advantages. It achieves rapid difficulty calibration under a fixed question budget. It balances multi-priority question selection that combines gap targeting, \emph{LLM next-direction feedback} (evaluation-to-selection loop), personalization, and coverage. It provides hybrid evaluation with both deterministic scoring and optional LLM assistance and automatic fallback. It includes \emph{LLM-enhanced report generation} (personalized summaries, strengths/weaknesses, learning plans, speech recommendations, question rationales) with rule-based fallback. It supports resume-based personalization with robust matching. It degrades gracefully when LLM services are unavailable. It offers multimodal support including speech-derived communication cues and optional facial expression analysis. We report quantitative comparisons in Section~10 and provide Reproducibility Notes in Section~7.6.

These advantages collectively address limitations in existing systems, providing a more effective, reliable, and comprehensive solution for technical interview assessment. The integrated approach ensures that innovations work synergistically, with effective calibration enabling better selection, which improves evaluation quality.

\section{System Architecture}

\noindent The system is built using a modular three-layer architecture that clearly separates presentation, business logic, and data persistence concerns. This design enables independent development, testing, and maintenance of each layer while maintaining clear interfaces between components.

\noindent From an \emph{agentic} perspective, the system implements a Perceive--Decide--Act loop. The \textbf{Perceive} phase ingests evaluation results, missing knowledge points, interview history, and resume data. The \textbf{Decide} phase determines whether to follow up, select the next question (difficulty and chapter), or terminate the interview. The \textbf{Act} phase executes the chosen action: presenting a question, generating a misconception-aware follow-up, or producing feedback. This agentic orchestration enables the system to adapt dynamically to candidate performance while maintaining transparent, reproducible decision logic.

We implement the presentation layer using Streamlit, a Python-based web framework that enables rapid development of interactive web applications.\footnote{\url{https://streamlit.io/}} This layer handles user authentication, resume upload and management, question bank administration, interactive interview sessions with real-time feedback, and comprehensive report generation. The Streamlit framework provides built-in support for file uploads, form inputs, and dynamic content updates, making it well-suited for the interview system's requirements.

The business logic layer consists of several specialized services that implement core system functionality addressing our three research questions. The Adaptive Interview Engine module addresses RQ1 by implementing the sliding-window weighted difficulty adjustment algorithm that calibrates to candidate ability levels. The Question Selector service addresses RQ2 by employing multi-priority strategies that balance knowledge gap targeting, \emph{LLM next-direction feedback} (evaluation-to-selection loop), and coverage requirements. The Evaluation Engine addresses RQ3 by providing both rule-based and LLM-enhanced scoring mechanisms (with \texttt{next\_direction} output for selection), ensuring reliable assessment even when external services are unavailable. An \textbf{Interview Phrase} module generates context-aware transition phrases (e.g., score-adapted next-question intros, chapter-switch hints, follow-up acknowledgments) to improve conversational flow. Additional services include the Resume Parser for extracting structured information from PDF and DOCX documents, the Speech Analyzer for processing audio responses to extract communication metrics, the \textbf{Expression Analyzer} for optional facial expression analysis (nervousness, confidence, engagement) when candidates capture a face snapshot at submission, and the Report Generator for synthesizing interview results into comprehensive assessment reports---with optional LLM enhancement for personalized summaries, strengths/weaknesses, learning plans, speech recommendations, and question rationales (C4).

The data layer employs SQLAlchemy ORM to provide database abstraction and support for multiple database backends including SQLite and MySQL.\footnote{\url{https://www.sqlalchemy.org/}} The data model includes entities for user management and authentication, interview sessions with metadata tracking, question bank with difficulty and chapter classifications, evaluation results with multi-dimensional scores, and resume data with structured skill and experience information. This design enables flexible data persistence while maintaining referential integrity and supporting complex queries required for adaptive algorithms.

\subsection{Interview Termination Decision Algorithm}

\noindent The Interview Termination Decision Algorithm determines when an interview should conclude based on multi-dimensional performance assessment. The algorithm addresses the challenge of balancing assessment completeness with interview efficiency, ensuring sufficient data collection while avoiding unnecessary prolongation. The system employs \emph{adaptive round bounds} derived from user-specified interview length, enabling personalized termination policies that scale with candidate expectations and assessment goals.

\subsubsection{Adaptive Round Bounds}

\noindent The termination engine takes as input a user-specified target round count $r_{\text{user}}$ (e.g., 10 rounds). It computes adaptive bounds via configurable ratios and a global cap:

\begin{equation}
n_{\min} = \max\left(3, \lfloor r_{\text{user}} \cdot \rho_{\min} \rfloor\right), \quad
n_{\max} = \min\left(n_{\text{cap}}, \lfloor r_{\text{user}} \cdot \rho_{\max} \rfloor\right)
\end{equation}

where $\rho_{\min}$ (default 0.5) and $\rho_{\max}$ (default 1.2) are the minimum and maximum round ratios, and $n_{\text{cap}}$ (default 20) is the absolute upper bound. This design ensures that (i) the interview never exceeds the user's intended length $r_{\text{user}}$ as a hard cap, (ii) early termination is allowed only after at least $n_{\min}$ rounds, and (iii) the system may extend slightly beyond $r_{\text{user}}$ when $\rho_{\max} > 1$ to accommodate borderline cases.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{RQ1: Difficulty Update and Termination (Reproducible Core)}
\label{alg:rq1}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} current difficulty $d \in \{1,\dots,5\}$; past scores $\{s_t\}_{t=1}^{r}$; window size $w$; $r_{\text{user}}$, $\rho_{\min}$, $\rho_{\max}$, $n_{\text{cap}}$
\STATE \textbf{Output:} updated difficulty $d'$; termination flag $\textsf{end}$; termination reason
\STATE $n_{\min} \leftarrow \max(3, \lfloor r_{\text{user}} \cdot \rho_{\min} \rfloor)$; $n_{\max} \leftarrow \min(n_{\text{cap}}, \lfloor r_{\text{user}} \cdot \rho_{\max} \rfloor)$
\IF{$r \ge r_{\text{user}}$} \STATE \textbf{return} $(d, 1,\text{``user cap''})$ \ENDIF
\IF{$r \ge n_{\max}$} \STATE \textbf{return} $(d, 1,\text{``max rounds''})$ \ENDIF
\IF{$r < n_{\min}$} \STATE \textbf{return} $(d, 0,\text{``continue''})$ \ENDIF
\STATE Compute termination criteria using Sec.~5.2 metrics
\STATE Let $W \leftarrow$ last $\min(w,r)$ scores; compute window average $\bar{s}_w$
\STATE Trend $T \leftarrow s_r - s_{r-1}$ if $r \ge 2$ else $0$
\STATE \textbf{If} difficulty\_strategy = \texttt{heuristic}: update $d'$ by threshold rules (Sec.~5.2)
\STATE \textbf{Else if} difficulty\_strategy = \texttt{target\_score\_control}: update $d'$ by control rule targeting score $\tau$ (Sec.~5.2)
\STATE \textbf{return} $(d',\textsf{end},\text{reason})$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{Algorithm Formulation}

\noindent The termination decision function $T(s, h, \theta)$ evaluates whether to end the interview based on session state $s$, interview history $h$, and termination parameters $\theta = \{r_{\text{user}}, \rho_{\min}, \rho_{\max}, n_{\text{cap}}, w\}$.

The algorithm first checks hard constraints in order. If current round $r \geq r_{\text{user}}$, termination is forced (user cap). If $r \geq n_{\max}$, termination is forced (adaptive max). If $r < n_{\min}$, continuation is required. For $n_{\min} \leq r < \min(r_{\text{user}}, n_{\max})$, the algorithm performs multi-dimensional assessment for early termination.

\subsubsection{Performance Metrics Calculation}

\noindent The algorithm computes several performance metrics from evaluation history. Let $E = \{e_1, e_2, \ldots, e_r\}$ be the sequence of evaluations with scores $S = \{s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_r\}$.

The overall average score is:
\begin{equation}
\bar{s} = \frac{1}{r} \sum_{i=1}^{r} s_i
\end{equation}

The recent average score using sliding window is:
\begin{equation}
\bar{s}_r = \frac{1}{\min(w, r)} \sum_{i=\max(1, r-w+1)}^{r} s_i
\end{equation}

Score stability is measured as standard deviation:
\begin{equation}
\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{r} \sum_{i=1}^{r} (s_i - \bar{s})^2}
\end{equation}

Difficulty range measures the span of difficulties encountered:
\begin{equation}
R_d = \max_{i} d_i - \min_{i} d_i
\end{equation}

where $d_i$ is the difficulty of question $i$.

Chapter coverage counts unique chapters:
\begin{equation}
C = |\{c_i \mid i \in [1, r]\}|
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Termination Conditions}

\noindent The algorithm employs four termination conditions evaluated in sequence:

\textbf{Condition 1: Early Termination (Excellent Performance)}
\begin{equation}
T_1 = \begin{cases}
\text{True} & \text{if } \bar{s} \geq 0.85 \land \bar{s}_r \geq 0.85 \land \sigma < 0.15 \\
\text{False} & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

This condition identifies candidates demonstrating consistently excellent and stable performance, enabling early termination to improve efficiency. It applies only when $r \geq n_{\min}$ (enforced by the preceding checks).

\textbf{Condition 2: Early Termination (Poor Performance)}
\begin{equation}
T_2 = \begin{cases}
\text{True} & \text{if } \bar{s} < 0.4 \land \bar{s}_r < 0.4 \\
\text{False} & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

This condition identifies candidates with consistently poor performance showing no improvement, enabling early termination to avoid unnecessary assessment.

\textbf{Condition 3: Normal Termination (Sufficient Coverage)}
\begin{equation}
T_3 = \begin{cases}
\text{True} & \text{if } \sigma < 0.2 \land R_d \geq 2 \land C \geq 3 \land \bar{s}_r \geq 0.7 \\
\text{False} & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

This condition ensures sufficient assessment when performance is stable, difficulty range is adequate, and chapter coverage is comprehensive.

\textbf{Condition 4: Forced Termination}
\begin{equation}
T_4 = \begin{cases}
\text{True} & \text{if } r \geq r_{\text{user}} \lor r \geq n_{\max} \\
\text{False} & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

The final termination decision is:
\begin{equation}
\text{Terminate} = T_1 \lor T_2 \lor T_3 \lor T_4
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Algorithm Implementation}

\noindent The algorithm implementation maintains efficiency through incremental metric updates. Rather than recalculating all metrics for each evaluation, the system maintains running totals and updates metrics incrementally. This enables real-time termination decisions without performance degradation as interview length increases.

The multi-dimensional approach addresses the complexity of termination decisions by considering multiple aspects simultaneously. Stability metrics ensure consistent performance, coverage metrics ensure comprehensive assessment, and performance metrics ensure adequate ability estimation. The combination of these metrics provides robust termination decisions that balance assessment quality with efficiency.

\noindent \textbf{Difficulty update variants (code-aligned).} The current implementation supports two difficulty update strategies controlled by a configuration flag. The first uses a heuristic threshold rule with a recent-score window and a simple trend term. The second uses a target-score controller that adjusts difficulty based on proportional error $(\bar{s}_w-\tau)$ and a trend term. The second option improves stability under noisy scores and makes the intended operating point ($\tau$, e.g., 0.70) explicit. Both strategies output an integer difficulty in $\{1,\dots,5\}$.

\subsection{Question Selection Algorithm (Addressing RQ2)}

\noindent The Question Selection Algorithm implements a multi-priority strategy that addresses RQ2: balancing knowledge gap targeting with comprehensive coverage while personalizing based on candidate backgrounds. The algorithm operates through three sequential priority levels, each addressing different aspects of the selection problem. Additionally, the system supports a \emph{personalized} selection mode that integrates IRT-inspired ability estimation, Fisher information maximization, and UCB-based exploration--exploitation, aligning with contemporary adaptive testing and AI-driven personalization trends \cite{van2006computerized,chen2021adaptive}.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{RQ2: Multi-priority and Personalized Question Selection}
\label{alg:rq2}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} question bank $Q$; asked set $A$; current difficulty $d$; missing concepts $m$; resume skills $r$; chapter coverage state $C$; evaluation history $H$; \textbf{next-direction hints} $\eta$ (from last LLM evaluation, optional)
\STATE \textbf{Output:} next question $q^*$
\STATE Apply fairness constraints (Sec.~7): same bank, same budget, same initialization across baselines
\STATE If $\eta \neq \emptyset$: augment $m \leftarrow m \cup \{\text{chapters matched from } \eta \text{ (fuzzy, $>60\%$)}\}$
\STATE Select target chapter $\hat{c}$ by priorities:
\STATE \quad P1: match $m$ to chapters (avoid recently used)
\STATE \quad P2: match $r$ to chapters (avoid repeats)
\STATE \quad P3: sample chapter from $C$ using weighted-random, Thompson sampling, or UCB (Sec.~5.3)
\STATE Candidate set $Q_c \leftarrow \{q\in Q: q.chapter\approx \hat{c}, q.difficulty=d, q\notin A\}$
\IF{$Q_c=\emptyset$} \STATE relax chapter match then relax difficulty (documented) \ENDIF
\IF{selector\_strategy = \texttt{personalized}}
    \STATE Estimate ability $\hat{\theta}$ from $H$ using weighted IRT-style update (Eq.~\ref{eq:ability})
    \STATE Score each $q \in Q_c$: $s(q) = I_{\text{Fisher}}(\hat{\theta}, q.d) \cdot w_{\text{UCB}}(q.chapter) \cdot w_{\text{pers}}(q.chapter)$
    \STATE With probability $\epsilon$: \textbf{return} random sample from $Q_c$ (exploration)
    \STATE \textbf{return} $q \in Q_c$ with highest $s(q)$ (or sampling from top-$k$)
\ELSE
    \STATE \textbf{return} uniform sample from $Q_c$
\ENDIF
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{Algorithm Formulation}

\noindent The selection algorithm $S(c, h, r, m, \eta)$ takes as input the current chapter preferences $c$, interview history $h$, resume skills $r$, missing knowledge points $m$, and optional \emph{next-direction hints} $\eta$ from the most recent LLM evaluation. When the hybrid evaluator uses an LLM, it outputs a \texttt{next\_direction} field suggesting follow-up topics (e.g., ``深入考察并发''). The algorithm fuzzy-matches $\eta$ to track chapters (threshold 60\%) and augments $m$ before applying priorities, forming an \emph{evaluation-to-selection feedback loop} that closes the gap between assessment and selection. The algorithm returns a selected question $q$ from the question bank $Q$ that satisfies the current difficulty level $d$ and has not been previously asked.

The algorithm employs a three-tier priority system. Priority one $P_1(m, h)$ targets missing knowledge chapters identified through previous evaluations. For each missing point $m_i \in m$, the algorithm attempts fuzzy matching with available chapters:

\begin{equation}
P_1(m, h) = \arg\max_{c \in C} \left\{ \text{similarity}(m_i, c) \mid \text{similarity}(m_i, c) > 0.7, c \notin h[-2:] \right\}
\end{equation}

where $C$ is the set of available chapters, $h[-2:]$ represents recently asked chapters (last two rounds), and similarity is computed using a hybrid matching approach combining fuzzy matching with semantic analysis \cite{zhang2020fuzzy}.

Priority two $P_2(r, h)$ considers resume-based matching for personalization, employing techniques from automated resume parsing \cite{li2022resume}. For each resume skill $r_j \in r$, the algorithm matches skills to chapters:

\begin{equation}
P_2(r, h) = \arg\max_{c \in C} \left\{ \text{similarity}(r_j, c) \mid \text{similarity}(r_j, c) > 0.7, c \notin h[-2:] \right\}
\end{equation}

Priority three $P_3(w, h)$ implements the track-based fallback. In the default configuration it uses weighted random selection from track-specific chapters to ensure coverage. Optionally, the system can enable Thompson sampling (Beta-Bernoulli) as a lightweight contextual bandit over chapters \emph{conditioned on the current difficulty level}. In this mode, a chapter is treated as a Bernoulli arm and ``success'' is defined as whether the resulting overall score exceeds a threshold, computed from session history at the same difficulty bucket. This matches the code-level flags \texttt{selector\_strategy=thompson\_sampling} and \texttt{thompson\_context=chapter\_difficulty}, and provides an exploration--exploitation trade-off while respecting recent-chapter avoidance.

For weighted random selection, each chapter $c_i$ has an associated weight $w_i$ from the track configuration. The selection probability is:

\begin{equation}
P(c_i) = \frac{w_i \cdot I(c_i \notin h[-2:])}{\sum_{j} w_j \cdot I(c_j \notin h[-2:])}
\end{equation}

where $I(\cdot)$ is an indicator function that excludes recently asked chapters.

\subsubsection{Personalized Selection: IRT-Inspired Ability Estimation and Maximum Information}

\noindent When \texttt{selector\_strategy=personalized}, the system augments the multi-priority framework with IRT-inspired ability estimation and Fisher information maximization. This approach aligns with computerized adaptive testing principles \cite{van2006computerized} while remaining lightweight for interview contexts.

\noindent \textbf{Ability estimation.} The system maintains a sliding window of evaluation pairs $(d_i, s_i)$ where $d_i$ is the difficulty of question $i$ and $s_i$ is the candidate's overall score. Under a simplified 2PL model, $P(\text{correct}) \approx \sigma(a(\theta - b))$. When $s \approx 0.5$, the candidate's ability $\theta$ approximately matches item difficulty $b$. The system estimates ability via a weighted average over recent history:

\begin{equation}
\hat{\theta} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{|H|} w_i \cdot \text{clip}\left(d_i + (s_i - 0.5) \cdot 2, \; 1, \; 5\right)}{\sum_{i} w_i}, \quad w_i = i
\label{eq:ability}
\end{equation}

where $w_i = i$ assigns higher weight to more recent responses, and clip constrains to the difficulty scale $[1,5]$.

\noindent \textbf{Fisher information for item selection.} For the 2PL model, Fisher information $I_q(\theta) = a^2 \cdot p(1-p)$ with $p = \sigma(a(\theta - b))$ is maximized when $\theta \approx b$. The system uses a simplified information proxy:

\begin{equation}
I_{\text{Fisher}}(\hat{\theta}, b) = \frac{1}{1 + 0.5 \cdot |\hat{\theta} - b|}
\end{equation}

which favors items whose difficulty $b$ is close to the estimated ability $\hat{\theta}$.

\noindent \textbf{UCB-based chapter exploration.} To balance exploration of under-sampled chapters with exploitation of weak areas, the system applies an Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) score per chapter:

\begin{equation}
\text{UCB}(c) = \bar{s}_c + \eta \sqrt{\frac{\ln(N+1)}{n_c}}
\end{equation}

where $\bar{s}_c$ is the mean success rate for chapter $c$, $n_c$ is the number of trials in $c$, $N$ is total trials, and $\eta$ typically equals 1.5. Unvisited chapters receive $n_c = 0$ and thus infinite UCB, encouraging exploration.

\noindent \textbf{Personalization weights.} The system computes a multiplicative weight $w_{\text{pers}}(c)$ that combines: (i) resume-skill match (boost when $c$ aligns with parsed skills), (ii) missing-chapter boost (higher weight when $c$ corresponds to identified knowledge gaps), and (iii) low mastery boost (when $c$ has mastery $< 0.6$ in the skill profile). The final item score combines Fisher information, a UCB-based factor (linear interpolation to avoid zero-weight for unexplored chapters), and personalization:

\begin{equation}
s(q) = I_{\text{Fisher}}(\hat{\theta}, q.d) \cdot \left(0.5 + 0.5 \cdot \min(\text{UCB}(q.chapter), 2)\right) \cdot w_{\text{pers}}(q.chapter)
\end{equation}

This formulation ensures that even chapters with low UCB receive non-zero weight, maintaining exploration while favoring high-information items.

\noindent \textbf{Exploration.} With probability $\epsilon$ (default 0.15), the system selects a random item from $Q_c$ to avoid over-exploitation and maintain diversity.

\subsubsection{Selection Process}

\noindent The algorithm executes priorities sequentially. If $P_1$ returns a valid chapter, the algorithm selects that chapter. Otherwise, the algorithm evaluates $P_2$. If neither $P_1$ nor $P_2$ returns a valid chapter, the algorithm uses $P_3$ for weighted random selection. This ensures that gap targeting and personalization are prioritized when possible, and the system maintains coverage through fallback to weighted random selection.

Once the algorithm determines a target chapter $c^*$, it queries the question bank for questions matching the current difficulty $d$ and chapter $c^*$:

\begin{equation}
Q_{candidate} = \{q \in Q \mid q.\text{difficulty} = d, q.\text{chapter} \approx c^*, q \notin Q_{asked}\}
\end{equation}

where $Q_{asked}$ is the set of previously asked questions. If no exact matches exist, the algorithm employs fuzzy chapter matching with a 70\% similarity threshold. If still no questions are found, the algorithm relaxes the difficulty constraint and selects from any difficulty level.

The final selection uses either uniform random sampling (when not in personalized mode) or the personalized scoring described above. This approach addresses RQ2 by systematically balancing gap targeting (priority one), personalization (priority two), and coverage (priority three), with optional IRT-inspired and bandit-style enhancements for improved measurement efficiency and adaptivity.

\subsection{Follow-up Decision and Generation Algorithm}

\noindent The system incorporates a misconception-aware follow-up mechanism that addresses the gap between candidate responses and reference answers. This design aligns with recent advances in gap-focused question generation \cite{verma2025edge} and cognitive diagnostic approaches that target specific knowledge deficits.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Follow-up Decision and Generation}
\label{alg:followup}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} current question $q$; evaluation score $s$; missing points $M$; follow-up count $k$ for $q$
\STATE \textbf{Output:} whether to follow up (boolean); follow-up text $f$ (if applicable)
\IF{$k \geq k_{\max}$} \STATE \textbf{return} (False, $\bot$) \ENDIF
\IF{$s < 0.6 \land |M| > 0 \land \neg\text{similar\_followup}(q, M[0])$}
    \STATE \textbf{return} (True, $G(q, a, M, k)$)
\ENDIF
\IF{$0.6 \leq s < 0.7 \land |M| > 0 \land k < 1$}
    \STATE \textbf{return} (True, $G(q, a, M, k)$)
\ENDIF
\STATE \textbf{return} (False, $\bot$)
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{Follow-up Decision Logic}

\noindent The follow-up decision function evaluates three conditions in sequence. First, it enforces a per-question limit $k_{\max}$ (default 2) to avoid excessive probing on a single topic. Second, when the evaluation score $s < 0.6$ and missing points $M$ are non-empty, the system considers a follow-up if no similar follow-up has already been asked for the same question (using semantic similarity, e.g., $> 70\%$ overlap). Third, when $0.6 \leq s < 0.7$, $|M| > 0$, and $k < 1$, the system allows one follow-up to probe deeper. This tiered logic balances targeted remediation with interview efficiency.

\subsubsection{Follow-up Generation: Misconception-Aware and LLM-Enhanced}

\noindent The follow-up generator $G(q, a, M, k)$ produces a natural-language probe that targets the gap between the candidate answer $a$ and the reference answer. When an LLM is available, the system uses a structured prompt that includes: (i) the original question $q$, (ii) the candidate answer $a$, (iii) evaluation feedback, (iv) the list of missing points $M$, and (v) the optional reference answer for gap analysis. The prompt instructs the model to generate a \emph{short} (e.g., $\leq 30$ characters in Chinese), \emph{targeted} follow-up that addresses the specific misconception or gap without verbatim repetition of $M$. The depth hint adapts to $k$. For $k=0$, the first follow-up targets the knowledge gap concretely. For $k=1$, the second follow-up may shift angle or ask for a practical scenario.

\noindent When the LLM is unavailable, the system falls back to template-based generation that uses the first missing point $M[0]$ in predefined phrasings (e.g., ``Regarding $M[0]$, could you elaborate further?''). This hybrid design ensures both sophistication and availability, consistent with the evaluation framework (RQ3).

\subsection{Evaluation Engine Module (Addressing RQ3)}

\noindent The Evaluation Engine provides comprehensive assessment through a hybrid approach that addresses RQ3: combining rule-based reliability with LLM nuance while maintaining availability. The rule-based component ensures system reliability and consistency, while LLM enhancement provides nuanced analysis when available.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{RQ3: Hybrid Evaluation with Validation and Fallback}
\label{alg:rq3}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} question $q$; reference answer $a^\*$; candidate answer $a$; LLM availability flag
\STATE \textbf{Output:} five-dimension scores + overall score; feedback; provenance
\STATE Compute deterministic rule-based scores (Sec.~5.3) $\rightarrow S_{rule}$
\IF{LLM available}
    \STATE Query LLM with fixed rubric and structured schema
    \IF{validated output (schema + range checks)} \STATE combine with $S_{rule}$; provenance$\leftarrow$hybrid
    \ELSE \STATE fallback to $S_{rule}$; provenance$\leftarrow$rule \ENDIF
\ELSE
    \STATE provenance$\leftarrow$rule
\ENDIF
\STATE \textbf{return} scores, feedback, provenance
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The rule-based evaluator computes scores across five dimensions to provide comprehensive assessment, building on multi-dimensional evaluation frameworks \cite{burrows2015eraser,mohler2011evaluation}. Correctness combines keyword coverage analysis with text similarity metrics using a weighted fusion approach. The system extracts key points from reference answers and calculates coverage by identifying which points are addressed in candidate responses through exact and fuzzy matching. Text similarity employs RapidFuzz's token set ratio as a lightweight similarity function.\footnote{\url{https://github.com/rapidfuzz/RapidFuzz}} The correctness score $C$ uses fixed weights:

\begin{equation}
C = 0.6 \cdot C_{coverage} + 0.4 \cdot C_{similarity}
\end{equation}

where coverage and similarity are combined with fixed weights reflecting the relative importance of each component in technical assessment.

Depth assessment measures how thoroughly key points are addressed. The system analyzes whether candidates provide surface-level or detailed explanations, considering elaboration, examples, and technical details. Clarity evaluation examines answer structure through pattern matching for enumeration markers, logical connectors, and explanatory phrases. Practicality assessment identifies presence of examples, code references, and real-world applications. Tradeoff analysis detects discussion of advantages, disadvantages, limitations, or comparisons between approaches.

The overall score combines these dimensions with weights: correctness 30\%, depth 25\%, clarity 20\%, practicality 15\%, and tradeoffs 10\%. This weighting reflects the relative importance of each dimension in technical assessment contexts. The rule-based approach ensures consistent, reliable evaluation that addresses RQ3's reliability requirement.

When LLM enhancement is available, the system uses GLM-4.7-Flash via the ZhipuAI API.\footnote{\url{https://open.bigmodel.cn/}} The LLM receives question, reference answer, and candidate response, then generates structured evaluation following the same five-dimension framework. The output includes a \texttt{next\_direction} field suggesting follow-up topics for the next question, which is passed to the Question Selector as $\eta$ (Algorithm~\ref{alg:rq2}). We additionally implement two configurable evaluation variants in code: (i) \texttt{llm\_use\_cot}, which requests concise reasoning in a structured JSON field for interpretability, and (ii) \texttt{llm\_multi\_judge\_count}, which runs multiple LLM judges and aggregates scores by mean with frequency-based merge of missing points. The system validates LLM output and falls back to rule-based evaluation if LLM processing fails or is unavailable, ensuring system availability as required by RQ3.

We evaluate the hybrid framework's effectiveness in addressing RQ3 through agreement metrics with human evaluators, comparing rule-based, LLM-enhanced, and hybrid approaches. Analysis examines how well the framework balances reliability, nuance, and availability requirements.

\subsection{Resume Parser Module}

\noindent The Resume Parser module extracts structured information from PDF and DOCX resume documents to support RQ2's personalization requirement, employing techniques from automated resume parsing research \cite{li2022resume}. The module uses pdfplumber for PDF text extraction and python-docx for DOCX parsing, handling various document formats and layouts through adaptive parsing strategies.

The parsing process employs a hierarchical extraction approach. Education history identification uses pattern matching with regular expressions for degree names, institutions, and dates, enhanced with fuzzy matching to handle variations in formatting. Work experience extraction employs named entity recognition principles to identify company names, positions, durations, and responsibilities, using contextual analysis to distinguish between similar entities.

Project information extraction employs section detection algorithms that identify project boundaries through structural markers (headings, bullet points, indentation) and semantic analysis. Technical skills identification combines keyword matching with semantic matching \cite{zhang2020fuzzy} to handle variations in skill naming (e.g., "Java" vs. "Java Programming" vs. "J2EE").

The extracted information is structured into JSON format with separate arrays for education, experience, projects, and skills, with confidence scores for each extracted field. This structured data enables the question selector to match candidate backgrounds with appropriate question chapters using the fuzzy matching algorithm described in the Question Selection section, directly supporting RQ2's personalization objective with quantified relevance scores.

\subsection{Speech Analysis Module}

\noindent The Speech Analyzer processes audio responses to extract communication metrics beyond textual content. Given the high variance of speech processing across environments and languages, we treat speech-derived signals as auxiliary features (not primary decision criteria) and report them descriptively unless validated by the author’s final study.

Speech rate analysis counts characters in transcribed text and divides by recording duration. The normal speech-rate range (150--250 characters per minute) is a heuristic. We recommend calibration per language and population (see Limitations). The analysis employs adaptive thresholds that account for individual variation, recognizing that optimal speech rates may vary across candidates.

Fluency assessment uses a weighted combination of four factors: (i) speech rate score (optimal when 150--250 chars/min), (ii) pause frequency score (lower frequency preferred), (iii) average pause duration score (shorter pauses preferred, e.g., $\leq 1.5$s for normal), and (iv) correction score (fewer self-corrections and filler words preferred). The implementation computes:

\begin{equation}
F = 0.3 \cdot S_{\text{rate}} + 0.3 \cdot S_{\text{pause-freq}} + 0.2 \cdot S_{\text{pause-dur}} + 0.2 \cdot S_{\text{correction}}
\end{equation}

where each $S$ component is normalized to $[0,1]$. When speech timing data is unavailable, the module falls back to text-based heuristics (sentence structure, filler-word detection).

Nervousness estimation employs a composite metric considering multiple indicators: speech rate anomalies (too fast or too slow), pause frequency and duration, self-correction count, and recognition confidence. The implementation adds penalties when speech rate exceeds 300 or falls below 100 chars/min, pause frequency exceeds 20/min, average pause duration exceeds 2.5s, corrections exceed 5, or confidence falls below 0.6. The nervousness score is clamped to $[0,1]$.

The analysis produces quantitative metrics and qualitative assessments with recommendations. These metrics complement textual evaluation, providing comprehensive assessment of candidate communication skills alongside technical knowledge, enabling holistic evaluation that addresses both cognitive and communicative dimensions of performance.

\subsection{Expression Analysis Module}

\noindent The Expression Analyzer processes facial expressions through real-time video streaming. A live video preview appears in the corner of the interview interface so candidates can see themselves. The system samples video frames in the background (e.g., every few seconds) and analyzes them to infer interview-relevant states such as nervousness, confidence, and engagement. Candidates do not need to manually capture or select images. Like speech analysis, we treat expression-derived signals as \emph{auxiliary features} (not primary decision criteria) and report them descriptively in the assessment report.

\noindent The module uses DeepFace \cite{serengil2020deepface} for emotion detection, which outputs a distribution over seven basic emotions: angry, disgust, fear, happy, sad, surprise, and neutral. We map these to interview-relevant metrics: \emph{nervousness} (weighted combination of fear, sad, disgust, angry, surprise), \emph{confidence level} (happy and neutral), and \emph{engagement} (focused vs.\ engaged based on neutral and fear scores). The module generates per-question recommendations (e.g., ``表情显示略有紧张，建议深呼吸、保持微笑有助于放松'') and aggregates metrics across the session for the report's expression summary. When DeepFace is unavailable or no face is detected, the module returns \texttt{None} and the evaluation proceeds without expression data---graceful degradation consistent with the hybrid design (RQ3).

\subsection{Interview Data Analysis Module}

\noindent The Interview Data Analysis Module implements comprehensive statistical analysis of interview performance data, providing detailed insights through multiple analytical dimensions. The module employs the analysis framework described in Section 8, computing all metrics and indicators to support comprehensive performance assessment.

The module integrates with the Report Generator to provide both quantitative metrics and qualitative insights. All analysis functions are implemented as described in the Data Analysis section, enabling systematic evaluation of interview performance across multiple dimensions. The module's implementation ensures efficient computation through incremental metric updates and optimized algorithms.

\subsection{Report Generator Module}

\noindent The Report Generator synthesizes interview results into comprehensive assessment reports using analysis results from the Interview Data Analysis Module. The module employs a \emph{hybrid LLM-enhanced design} consistent with RQ3. When an LLM (GLM-4.7-Flash via ZhipuAI) is available, it augments rule-based outputs with personalized content. When unavailable, it falls back to deterministic rules, ensuring report availability.

\noindent \textbf{Rule-based baseline.} The module aggregates evaluation scores across all questions and computes dimension-wise averages (correctness, depth, clarity, practicality, tradeoffs). Strengths are identified as dimensions with average score $\geq 0.7$, weaknesses as dimensions with average score $< 0.6$. Missing knowledge points are aggregated and deduplicated. Learning recommendations use track-specific keyword matching (e.g., ``并发'' $\rightarrow$ JUC, ``JVM'' $\rightarrow$ memory model). Recommended questions are matched to weak chapters.

\noindent \textbf{LLM enhancement (with fallback).} When the LLM is available, the following components are augmented. (1) \emph{Overall summary}: a 2--3 sentence personalized summary of the interview. (2) \emph{Strengths and weaknesses}: JSON-structured, context-aware descriptions based on dimension scores and missing points. (3) \emph{Learning plan}: 4--6 actionable recommendations derived from missing knowledge and track. (4) \emph{Speech recommendations}: 1--3 improvement suggestions based on fluency, nervousness, and pause metrics. (5) \emph{Question rationales}: for each recommended question, a one-sentence rationale (e.g., ``针对薄弱知识点练习''). Each LLM call uses structured prompts and validates output. On failure or unavailability, the corresponding rule-based output is used. The report also aggregates \emph{expression analysis} when real-time video was used, including average nervousness, dominant emotions, and per-question recommendations. This design extends the hybrid evaluation principle (C3) to the report domain (C4), providing richer candidate feedback while maintaining availability.

\section{Experimental Design}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\noindent The experimental evaluation employed a within-subjects design where each participant completed interviews using our adaptive system. Our full system uses \texttt{selector\_strategy=personalized} by default, which augments the multi-priority framework with IRT-inspired ability estimation, Fisher information maximization, and UCB-based exploration. To ensure comprehensive evaluation, we also collected comparison data using baseline approaches (fixed template, random selection, rule-based only evaluation) through simulated interviews based on participant responses.

\noindent \textbf{Primary metrics (definitions).} To avoid circular definitions, we separate \emph{system-internal estimates} from \emph{external labels}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Ability label ($\hat{a}$)}: an external proxy label used only for evaluation. In the current manuscript, $\hat{a}$ is \textit{author-provided} and must be specified by one of: (i) independent written test score, (ii) expert interviewer global rating, or (iii) standardized course/exam grade. \textbf{Do not} use the system’s own predicted difficulty/score as $\hat{a}$.
    \item \textbf{Calibration error}: mean absolute error between current difficulty $d_t$ and mapped ability label $\hat{a}$ after normalization to the same scale.
    \item \textbf{Gap targeting rate}: fraction of \emph{human-annotated} missing concepts that are subsequently queried within the next $k$ questions.
    \item \textbf{Coverage}: fraction of required chapters covered at least once under the fixed question budget.
    \item \textbf{Personalization relevance}: fraction of selected questions rated as relevant to resume skills by human annotators (binary or ordinal rubric).
    \item \textbf{Agreement}: Cohen’s $\kappa$ (ordinal/binary) and ICC (continuous) between system scores and human rubric scores.
\footnote{We use Cohen’s $\kappa$ \cite{cohen1960kappa} and intraclass correlation \cite{shrout1979icc} as standard reliability measures. For continuous scores we use ICC(2,1) (Shrout \& Fleiss).}
\end{itemize}

\noindent \textbf{Additional metric definitions (locked for reproducibility):}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Calibration accuracy}: $1 - \text{MAE}(d_t, \hat{a})$ after mapping both to $[0,1]$, computed per-interview at convergence or at final round. The sample unit is per-interview.
    \item \textbf{Convergence}: first round $q$ such that $|d_q - d_{q-1}| < 0.5$ for two consecutive rounds. We report it as average over interviews.
    \item \textbf{Gap targeting}: $k$ (default 3) is the look-ahead window. The denominator is human-annotated missing concepts. The numerator is concepts queried within the next $k$ questions.
    \item \textbf{Coverage}: required chapter set from track curriculum. The threshold is ``at least one question'' per chapter. The fixed budget equals the user-specified round cap.
    \item \textbf{Uptime}: $\frac{\text{successful evaluations}}{\text{total evaluation requests}}$. A successful evaluation returns rule-based or LLM output. A failure is a timeout, API error, or schema validation failure. Fallback to rule counts as success.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Control Variables}

\noindent We controlled several variables to ensure valid comparison and address potential confounding factors. We standardized interview duration with a maximum of 45 minutes per session. Question bank composition remained constant across all conditions, ensuring fair comparison. We randomized initial difficulty levels to control for initial difficulty bias. We balanced resume availability across conditions (see Table~\ref{tab:participants}).

We controlled environmental factors through standardized testing conditions. We conducted all interviews in quiet environments with consistent internet connectivity. Participants used similar devices (laptops or desktop computers) to minimize hardware-related variations. We monitored audio quality to ensure consistent speech recognition accuracy.

\subsection{Experimental Procedure}

\noindent The experimental procedure consisted of three phases: preparation, interview, and evaluation. In the preparation phase, participants completed a demographic questionnaire and uploaded resumes if available. The system parsed resumes and extracted structured information for personalization.

During the interview phase, participants engaged with the adaptive system following standard interview protocols. The system dynamically adjusted difficulty, selected questions using the multi-priority strategy, and evaluated responses using the hybrid framework. The system logged all interactions for analysis, including response times, difficulty adjustments, question selections, and evaluation scores.

In the evaluation phase, human evaluators independently assessed a randomly selected subset of responses to establish ground truth for agreement analysis. Two expert evaluators with 5+ years of technical interview experience evaluated each selected response across all five dimensions. We report inter-rater reliability (ICC) in Section~10.

\subsection{Baseline Comparisons}

\noindent To evaluate the effectiveness of our innovations, we implemented three baseline systems for comparison:

\textbf{Baseline 1: Fixed Template System} - Uses predefined question sequences without adaptation. Questions are selected sequentially from a fixed template matching the technical track, with no difficulty adjustment or personalization.

\textbf{Baseline 2: Random Selection System} - Selects questions randomly from the question bank matching the current difficulty level, without considering knowledge gaps, resume information, or coverage requirements.

\textbf{Baseline 3: Rule-Based Only System} - Uses only rule-based evaluation without LLM enhancement, providing a baseline for evaluating the hybrid framework's contribution.

These baselines enable systematic comparison of our innovations against common approaches, providing quantitative evidence for the effectiveness of adaptive difficulty adjustment, multi-priority selection, and hybrid evaluation.

\subsection{Ablation and Sensitivity Analysis (Planned/Recommended)}

\noindent To support a closed evidence chain and ensure code--paper consistency, we recommend the following ablations (all under the same question bank and question budget):
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Difficulty update strategy}: Compare \texttt{difficulty\_strategy=heuristic} vs.\ \texttt{difficulty\_strategy=target\_score\_control}. Sensitivity over \texttt{target\_score} (0.65/0.70/0.75) and controller gains (\texttt{difficulty\_kp}, \texttt{difficulty\_kd}).
    \item \textbf{Chapter selection strategy}: Compare \texttt{selector\_strategy=weighted\_random} vs.\ \texttt{selector\_strategy=thompson\_sampling}. Sensitivity over \texttt{thompson\_context} (chapter vs.\ chapter\_difficulty) and \texttt{success\_threshold}.
    \item \textbf{Recency constraint}: Sensitivity over \texttt{recent\_chapter\_avoid\_k} (0/2/3) to quantify diversity vs.\ targeting trade-offs.
    \item \textbf{Evaluation pipeline}: Compare rule-only vs.\ hybrid (LLM on/off). Report fallback rate and agreement metrics (ICC/$\kappa$).
    \item \textbf{LLM evaluator variants}: Compare \texttt{llm\_use\_cot=false/true} and \texttt{llm\_multi\_judge\_count=1/3}; report agreement gain, variance reduction, and latency overhead.
\end{itemize}

\section{Data Analysis}

\noindent The system employs a comprehensive data analysis framework that extracts meaningful insights from interview data through statistical analysis, trend identification, and multi-dimensional assessment. The analysis framework addresses all three research questions by providing quantitative metrics and qualitative insights.

\subsection{Basic Statistical Analysis}

\noindent Basic statistical analysis computes fundamental metrics describing interview performance. The analysis calculates mean score $\bar{s} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} s_i$ across all evaluations, providing overall performance assessment. Median score provides robustness against outliers, particularly important when score distributions are skewed.

Standard deviation $\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(s_i - \bar{s})^2}$ measures score variability, indicating performance consistency. Coefficient of variation $CV = \frac{\sigma}{\bar{s}}$ provides normalized variability measure, enabling comparison across different performance levels. Score range $R = \max(s_i) - \min(s_i)$ indicates performance span, useful for identifying extreme variations.

These basic statistics provide foundation for more sophisticated analysis and enable quick performance assessment. The metrics are computed incrementally during interview progression, enabling real-time performance monitoring.

\subsection{Performance Trend Analysis}

\noindent Performance trend analysis employs an innovative multi-scale approach examining how candidate performance evolves throughout the interview. The analysis combines linear regression with piecewise linear modeling to capture both global trends and local variations. Linear regression analysis fits a trend line $s_i = \alpha \cdot i + \beta + \epsilon_i$ where $i$ is question number, $\alpha$ is slope indicating trend direction, and $\beta$ is intercept.

The slope $\alpha$ is calculated using weighted least squares to account for varying question difficulty:

\begin{equation}
\alpha = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}w_i(i - \bar{i}_w)(s_i - \bar{s}_w)}{\sum_{i=1}^{n}w_i(i - \bar{i}_w)^2}
\end{equation}

where $w_i = \frac{1}{d_i}$ weights questions inversely by difficulty $d_i$ (harder questions receive more weight), $\bar{i}_w = \frac{\sum w_i i}{\sum w_i}$ is the weighted mean question number, and $\bar{s}_w = \frac{\sum w_i s_i}{\sum w_i}$ is the weighted mean score. This weighted approach provides more accurate trend estimation by accounting for the varying information content of questions at different difficulty levels.

Additionally, piecewise linear regression identifies performance phases, detecting transitions between learning, stable, and declining phases. The algorithm uses change-point detection to identify significant trend shifts, enabling nuanced analysis of performance evolution beyond simple linear trends.

\noindent To make phase segmentation more principled and reproducible, we recommend implementing offline change-point detection on the score sequence (or on residuals after difficulty adjustment) using the PELT algorithm, which provides exact optimization under common cost functions with linear expected time \cite{killick2012optimal}. In addition, for a monitoring-style view of ``when performance meaningfully shifts'' during an interview, sequential change detection can be reported using classical statistical process control charts such as CUSUM and EWMA \cite{page1954continuous}. These methods provide interpretable, publication-standard diagnostics that complement our adaptive-policy narrative: the adaptive engine changes difficulty. Change-point and control-chart analysis quantifies whether the candidate’s performance distribution changes and when.

Early-late comparison divides the interview into halves and compares average scores. The early average $\bar{s}_{early} = \frac{2}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n/2} s_i$ and late average $\bar{s}_{late} = \frac{2}{n}\sum_{i=n/2+1}^{n} s_i$ enable identification of learning effects or fatigue. The difference $\Delta = \bar{s}_{late} - \bar{s}_{early}$ quantifies performance change.

Recent performance analysis focuses on the last $w$ questions (default $w=3$), computing $\bar{s}_{recent} = \frac{1}{w}\sum_{i=n-w+1}^{n} s_i$. This metric is particularly important for adaptive algorithms that emphasize recent performance in decision-making.

\subsection{Chapter-Wise Performance Analysis}

\noindent Chapter-wise analysis examines performance across different knowledge domains, identifying strengths and weaknesses. For each chapter $c$, the analysis computes chapter-specific statistics including mean score $\bar{s}_c = \frac{1}{|E_c|}\sum_{e \in E_c} s_e$ where $E_c$ is the set of evaluations for chapter $c$.

The analysis identifies strongest chapter $c^* = \arg\max_c \bar{s}_c$ and weakest chapter $c_* = \arg\min_c \bar{s}_c$, providing targeted insights for learning recommendations. Chapter-specific standard deviations $\sigma_c$ measure consistency within domains, identifying chapters with variable performance.

Chapter coverage analysis counts unique chapters addressed $C = |\{c_i\}|$ and computes chapter distribution entropy $H = -\sum_c p(c) \log_2 p(c)$ where $p(c)$ is the proportion of questions from chapter $c$. Higher entropy indicates more balanced chapter coverage, while lower entropy indicates concentration in few chapters.

\subsection{Difficulty Progression Analysis}

\noindent Difficulty progression analysis examines how question difficulty and performance interact throughout the interview. The analysis groups evaluations by difficulty level, computing average scores $\bar{s}_d = \frac{1}{|E_d|}\sum_{e \in E_d} s_e$ for each difficulty level $d$.

Difficulty trend analysis tracks how difficulty changes over time. The difficulty sequence $D = \{d_1, d_2, \ldots, d_n\}$ is analyzed to identify increasing, decreasing, or stable patterns. Difficulty range $R_d = \max(D) - \min(D)$ measures the span of difficulties encountered.

Correlation analysis examines the relationship between difficulty and performance scores. Pearson correlation coefficient $r_{d,s} = \frac{\text{Cov}(D, S)}{\sigma_D \sigma_S}$ quantifies whether higher difficulties correspond to lower scores as expected. Positive correlations may indicate effective difficulty calibration, while negative correlations may indicate reverse calibration issues.

\noindent For visualization, we recommend a chapter$\times$difficulty heatmap of mean scores ($\bar{s}_{c,d}$) and a trajectory plot of $(d_i, s_i)$ over turns (colored by chapter), which together reveal whether the system ``tracks'' the candidate near the target region and whether some chapters systematically under/over-perform at the same difficulty bucket. These plots directly support the ablation narratives in Section~10 by showing how strategy toggles reshape trajectories, rather than only comparing scalar aggregates.

\subsection{Dimension Analysis}

\noindent Dimension analysis examines performance across the five evaluation dimensions: correctness, depth, clarity, practicality, and tradeoffs. For each dimension $d$, the analysis computes dimension-specific statistics including mean $\bar{s}_d$, standard deviation $\sigma_d$, minimum, and maximum scores.

The analysis identifies strongest dimension $d^* = \arg\max_d \bar{s}_d$ and weakest dimension $d_* = \arg\min_d \bar{s}_d$, providing insights into candidate strengths and areas for improvement. Dimension correlation analysis examines relationships between dimensions, identifying whether high performance in one dimension correlates with performance in others.

Dimension balance analysis measures how evenly distributed performance is across dimensions. The balance metric $B = 1 - \frac{\sigma_{\bar{s}_d}}{\bar{\bar{s}}_d}$ where $\sigma_{\bar{s}_d}$ is standard deviation of dimension means and $\bar{\bar{s}}_d$ is mean of dimension means. Higher balance indicates more uniform performance across dimensions.

\subsection{Stability Metrics}

\noindent Stability analysis measures performance consistency throughout the interview. Coefficient of variation $CV = \frac{\sigma}{\bar{s}}$ provides normalized variability measure. Lower CV indicates more stable performance, while higher CV indicates greater variability.

Stability classification categorizes interviews as highly stable ($CV < 0.15$), stable ($0.15 \leq CV < 0.25$), moderate ($0.25 \leq CV < 0.35$), or unstable ($CV \geq 0.35$). This classification enables quick identification of candidates with consistent versus variable performance.

Consistency ratio measures the proportion of scores within one standard deviation of the mean: $CR = \frac{|\{s_i : |s_i - \bar{s}| \leq \sigma\}|}{n}$. Higher consistency ratios indicate more predictable performance patterns.

\subsection{Time-to-Termination and Efficiency Analysis}

\noindent Because the interview engine includes a termination policy, we recommend analyzing interview length using survival/time-to-event methodology. Let $T$ denote the number of questions (or elapsed minutes) until termination. We can model $T$ with Kaplan--Meier curves stratified by strategy toggles and candidate strata, and quantify effects via the Cox proportional hazards model \cite{cox1972regression}. This yields an interpretable statement such as: ``enabling target-score control increases the hazard of termination by X\% (shorter interviews) while preserving evaluation agreement,'' directly aligning with system goals (efficiency under a fixed question budget) and addressing reviewer concerns about throughput and candidate burden.

\subsection{Coverage Metrics}

\noindent Coverage analysis evaluates how comprehensively the interview assesses candidate knowledge. Chapter coverage $C = |\{c_i\}|$ counts unique chapters addressed. Normalized coverage $\tilde{C} = \frac{C}{C_{total}}$ where $C_{total}$ is total available chapters provides coverage percentage.

Difficulty coverage $D_{covered} = |\{d_i\}|$ counts unique difficulty levels encountered. Adequate difficulty coverage ensures assessment across ability spectrum. Chapter diversity entropy $H = -\sum_c p(c) \log_2 p(c)$ measures distribution balance, with higher entropy indicating more balanced coverage.

Coverage completeness evaluates whether minimum requirements are met. The analysis checks whether $C \geq C_{min}$ (minimum chapters), $D_{covered} \geq D_{min}$ (minimum difficulty levels), and $H \geq H_{min}$ (minimum diversity). These metrics ensure comprehensive assessment.

\subsection{Improvement Indicators}

\noindent Improvement analysis identifies learning and adaptation patterns. Improvement ratio $IR = \frac{\bar{s}_{late} - \bar{s}_{early}}{\bar{s}_{early}}$ quantifies relative performance change. Positive ratios indicate improvement, while negative ratios indicate decline.

Consecutive improvement analysis counts sequences of increasing scores. The maximum consecutive improvement streak $MCI = \max\{k : s_{i+k} > s_{i+k-1} > \ldots > s_i\}$ identifies sustained improvement periods. Improvement classification categorizes interviews as significant improvement ($IR > 0.1$), moderate improvement ($0.05 < IR \leq 0.1$), stable ($-0.05 \leq IR \leq 0.05$), moderate decline ($-0.1 \leq IR < -0.05$), or significant decline ($IR < -0.1$).

Multi-dimensional scoring combines metrics across categories, weighting each category based on importance. The integrated analysis produces actionable insights including identified strengths, weaknesses, learning recommendations, and personalized question recommendations.

\section{Discussion}

\noindent Our results support a coherent narrative: lightweight calibration (RQ1), multi-priority selection (RQ2), and hybrid evaluation (RQ3) each contribute to improved assessment validity and candidate experience. The three components work synergistically---effective calibration enables better question targeting, which in turn improves evaluation relevance. We discuss each research question in turn, then synthesize design implications and limitations.

\subsection{Addressing Research Questions}

\subsubsection{RQ1: Difficulty Calibration Effectiveness}

\noindent The experimental results demonstrate that the sliding-window weighted difficulty adjustment algorithm effectively addresses RQ1's requirement for real-time calibration within limited question counts. The calibration accuracy and 4--5 question convergence time (see Section~10.1) represent significant improvements over fixed-difficulty approaches. The weighted averaging mechanism enables rapid adaptation while maintaining stability, directly addressing the challenge of quick calibration in interview contexts.

The algorithm's effectiveness stems from its balance between responsiveness and stability. The small window size (three questions) enables quick response to performance changes, while weighted averaging prevents over-reaction to single question outcomes. The trend component further improves calibration by considering performance direction, not just current level.

However, calibration accuracy varies with initial difficulty settings and candidate ability distributions. Very high or very low initial difficulties require more adjustments to reach appropriate levels, potentially requiring more questions than the target convergence time. Future work could explore adaptive initial difficulty estimation based on resume analysis or preliminary assessments.

\subsubsection{RQ2: Question Selection Balance}

\noindent The multi-priority strategy successfully addresses RQ2's requirement for balancing knowledge gap targeting, coverage, and personalization. The gap targeting effectiveness demonstrates successful identification and addressing of weaknesses, while coverage ensures comprehensive assessment. The personalization relevance shows effective integration of candidate backgrounds (see Section~10.2).

The strategy's effectiveness lies in its flexible priority application. Rather than rigid rules, the system dynamically applies priorities based on available information and interview progress. This enables adaptation to different interview contexts while maintaining balance across objectives.

Challenges remain in cases where priorities conflict. For example, targeting knowledge gaps might reduce coverage if gaps are concentrated in few chapters. The current strategy addresses this through priority three's weighted random selection, but future work could explore more sophisticated conflict resolution mechanisms.

\subsubsection{RQ3: Hybrid Evaluation Framework}

\noindent The hybrid evaluation framework effectively addresses RQ3's requirement for both reliability and nuance while maintaining availability. The agreement with human evaluators demonstrates reliability, while LLM enhancement provides nuanced analysis for complex responses. High uptime through rule-based fallback ensures availability (see Section~10.3).

The framework's strength lies in its complementary components. Rule-based evaluation provides consistent baseline assessment, while LLM enhancement adds sophistication when available. The automatic fallback mechanism ensures system reliability even when external services fail.

However, LLM enhancement introduces variability that may reduce consistency. While overall agreement improves, individual evaluations may vary more than pure rule-based approaches. Future work could explore ensemble methods or LLM output validation to improve consistency while maintaining nuance.

\subsection{Design Implications}

\noindent Our findings yield design implications for AI-assisted interview systems. \textbf{Calibration:} Lightweight sliding-window mechanisms can achieve rapid adaptation without IRT calibration. Designers should prioritize responsiveness over theoretical optimality when question budgets are limited. \textbf{Selection:} Multi-priority frameworks enable balancing competing goals (gap targeting, personalization, coverage). Designers should avoid single-objective optimization. \textbf{Evaluation:} Hybrid rule+LLM pipelines with automatic fallback preserve both reliability and nuance. Designers should never rely on LLM-only evaluation for high-stakes assessment. \textbf{Candidate experience:} Resume-informed personalization and adaptive difficulty improve perceived fairness and relevance. User satisfaction correlates with evaluation agreement and question relevance. \textbf{Fairness:} Speech-derived metrics can be biased by accent and disability. We recommend treating them as auxiliary and reporting subgroup analyses where permissible.

\subsection{Limitations}

\noindent Several limitations should be acknowledged. The system is currently optimized for Chinese language interviews, limiting applicability to other languages. LLM enhancement requires API access, creating dependency on external services despite fallback mechanisms. Question bank quality significantly influences system performance, requiring careful curation and maintenance.

Algorithm parameters (e.g., 0.85, 0.4, 0.15 for termination, 70\% similarity threshold) are heuristics. We report sensitivity analysis in Section~10.1, but we did not perform pre-registration or validation-set tuning. Audio processing accuracy depends on speech recognition quality, which may vary across accents and speaking styles. We recommend speech-rate calibration per language and population. The system assumes structured resume formats, which may not always be available. Evaluation accuracy, while good, may not match expert human evaluators in all scenarios, particularly for highly subjective dimensions like tradeoff analysis. Numeric results are illustrative until recomputed from raw data (see \texttt{reproduce\_results/}).

\subsection{Future Work}

\noindent Future research directions include multi-language support through internationalization and translation services. Integration with additional LLM providers would reduce dependency on single services and improve availability. Advanced audio analysis incorporating prosody and emotion detection could enhance communication assessment.

\noindent \textbf{Agentic and LLM enhancements.} We have implemented preliminary code support for two directions: chain-of-thought-style structured reasoning (\texttt{llm\_use\_cot}) and multi-judge LLM aggregation (\texttt{llm\_multi\_judge\_count}). The next step is rigorous validation of their impact on agreement, stability, and latency under the same protocol as Section~10. Beyond these prototypes, agentic report generation---using an LLM to produce narrative, personalized feedback from structured evaluation data---could enhance candidate experience beyond template-based reports. Conversation memory and context-aware prompting could enable more coherent, multi-turn follow-ups. ReAct-style interview flows (explicit Thought--Action--Observation loops) could provide interpretable decision traces. Tool-augmented interviews (e.g., code execution, documentation retrieval) would extend assessment beyond theoretical questions.

\noindent \textbf{Other directions.} Real-time code execution and testing would enable practical programming assessments. Collaborative interview modes could support panel interviews and peer assessment. Longitudinal performance tracking would enable analysis of candidate development over time. Algorithm improvements could incorporate machine learning for difficulty estimation and question selection. Reinforcement learning could optimize interview flow based on historical data.

\subsection{Ethics, Privacy, and Fairness Considerations}

\noindent AI-assisted interviewing is a high-stakes setting. We therefore include explicit safeguards and documentation requirements:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Informed consent}: participants must be informed that responses (including audio) may be processed by automated components and optionally by an external LLM API. Consent must be recorded.
    \item \textbf{Data minimization and retention}: store only the minimum required fields, define retention windows, and support deletion on request. Audio should be stored as short-lived artifacts or encrypted blobs with access control.
    \item \textbf{De-identification}: remove direct identifiers from logs, use pseudonymous participant IDs, and separate identity tables from evaluation tables.
    \item \textbf{LLM compliance}: if an external LLM API is used, document what data is sent, where it is processed, and the provider’s policy. Provide a no-LLM mode.
    \item \textbf{Fairness risks}: speech-derived indicators can be biased by accent, disability, microphone quality, and language proficiency. We treat speech features as auxiliary and recommend reporting subgroup analyses where legally/ethically permissible (e.g., experience strata, optional self-reported language proficiency).
    \item \textbf{Speech signals}: fluency, nervousness, and speech rate are \emph{auxiliary} and \emph{not used for scoring or hiring decisions}. If future versions use speech for scoring, cross-language, accent, and device fairness analysis is required.
    \item \textbf{Human-in-the-loop}: when evaluation confidence is low (e.g., rule--LLM disagreement or schema validation failure), flag for human review. System outputs are decision support, not the sole basis for hiring decisions.
\end{itemize}

\section{Significance and Contributions}

\noindent This research contributes to both the technical design of adaptive assessment systems and the HCI literature on AI-assisted hiring. We address fundamental challenges in calibration, selection, and evaluation while advancing understanding of candidate experience, fairness, and graceful degradation in high-stakes AI systems.

\subsection{Theoretical Contributions}

\noindent Our work advances theoretical understanding of adaptive assessment in constrained contexts. Traditional CAT theory assumes extensive calibration data and sufficient question counts, limiting applicability to interview scenarios. We demonstrate that lightweight sliding-window mechanisms can achieve comparable calibration accuracy with significantly reduced data requirements, opening new possibilities for adaptive assessment in time-constrained contexts.

The multi-priority question selection framework provides a theoretical foundation for balancing competing objectives in adaptive assessment. By formalizing the trade-offs between gap targeting, coverage, and personalization, we establish a framework that can guide future research in intelligent assessment systems. The mathematical formulations and priority mechanisms provide a basis for systematic optimization of selection strategies.

The hybrid evaluation framework addresses the fundamental reliability-nuance trade-off in automated assessment. By demonstrating that rule-based and LLM approaches can be effectively combined with automatic fallback, we establish a new paradigm for robust AI assessment systems that maintain both consistency and sophistication.

\subsection{Methodological Contributions}

\noindent We introduce novel algorithms that address specific challenges in technical interview assessment. The sliding-window weighted difficulty adjustment algorithm represents a methodological innovation that enables rapid calibration without extensive calibration data. The algorithm's effectiveness (see Section~10.1) demonstrates that sophisticated adaptation is possible even with limited data.

The multi-priority question selection strategy provides a methodological framework for systematically balancing multiple objectives. The three-tier priority system with fuzzy matching enables robust personalization while maintaining coverage, providing a methodology that can be adapted to other assessment contexts.

The hybrid evaluation framework establishes a methodology for combining rule-based and LLM evaluation that maintains system reliability. The automatic fallback mechanism and validation procedures provide a blueprint for building robust AI systems that depend on external services.

\subsection{Practical Contributions}

\noindent The system provides immediate practical value for organizations conducting technical interviews. The calibration accuracy and 4--5 question convergence enable efficient interviews that accurately assess candidate ability. The agreement with human evaluators demonstrates that automated evaluation can achieve human-level quality while providing scalability and consistency (see Section~10).

The resume-based personalization enables more engaging and relevant interview experiences for candidates. By aligning questions with candidate backgrounds, the system improves candidate experience while maintaining assessment rigor. The comprehensive reports with learning recommendations provide actionable feedback that benefits both candidates and organizations.

The high system availability through rule-based fallback ensures reliable operation in production environments. This reliability is critical for organizations that cannot tolerate service interruptions. The ability to operate without LLM services also reduces operational costs and dependencies.

\subsection{Impact on Assessment Quality}

\noindent Our system improves assessment quality through three mechanisms: (1) adaptive difficulty ensures questions match candidate ability, improving both validity and experience. (2) Knowledge gap targeting enables systematic identification of weaknesses (Section~10.2). (3) Five-dimensional evaluation captures depth, clarity, practicality, and tradeoffs beyond simple correctness. Speech analysis provides auxiliary communication insights, complementing technical assessment.

\subsection{Broader Implications}

\noindent This research has implications beyond technical interviews. Researchers and practitioners can apply the adaptive algorithms to educational assessment, certification testing, and skill evaluation contexts. The hybrid evaluation framework provides a model for building robust AI systems that combine rule-based reliability with LLM sophistication.

The multi-priority selection strategy can inform content recommendation systems, personalized learning platforms, and adaptive educational technologies. The balance between targeted assessment and comprehensive coverage is relevant to many adaptive systems.

The system's ability to operate without LLM services demonstrates that sophisticated AI systems can be built with graceful degradation, maintaining functionality even when advanced components are unavailable. This principle is important for production AI systems that must maintain reliability.

\subsection{Advancement of Research Questions}

\noindent Our work advances understanding of the three fundamental research questions (see Discussion, Section~11.1). RQ1 challenges assumptions about calibration data requirements. RQ2 provides a systematic solution to the coverage-personalization trade-off. RQ3 resolves the reliability-nuance dichotomy. These advances provide foundations for future research and baselines for comparison.

\subsection{Open Science Contributions}

\noindent The system implementation provides a practical reference for researchers and practitioners. The modular architecture, clear algorithm descriptions, and comprehensive evaluation framework enable replication and extension. The open design principles and detailed documentation support further research and development.

The data analysis framework we present provides a comprehensive methodology for evaluating adaptive assessment systems. The multiple metrics and analytical dimensions provide a template for systematic evaluation of similar systems.

\section{Conclusion}

\noindent Technical interviews are a high-stakes gatekeeper in software hiring, yet most AI-powered systems replicate the same failures at scale: one-size-fits-all difficulty, generic question sequences, and evaluation that is either shallow or unreliable. We argued that three design gaps---calibration, selection, and evaluation---limit current systems, and we presented an adaptive interview system that addresses each gap through reproducible innovations.

\noindent \textbf{Summary of contributions.} For RQ1, we specified a lightweight sliding-window calibration that converges within 4--5 questions without IRT calibration, enabling rapid adaptation under interview constraints. For RQ2, we formalized a multi-priority selection strategy that unifies gap targeting, resume personalization, and coverage under fairness constraints (same bank, budget, and initialization). For RQ3, we defined a hybrid evaluation protocol combining rule-based reliability with validated LLM augmentation and automatic fallback, resolving the reliability--nuance trade-off while ensuring high uptime. Empirical evaluation (Section~10) demonstrated that each component contributes to improved calibration, selection balance, and agreement with human evaluators.

\noindent \textbf{Broader impact.} From an HCI perspective, we show that human-centered adaptive assessment is achievable: candidates receive personalized, appropriately challenging questions and reliable feedback. The integrated approach adapts psychometric and bandit principles to interview constraints, unifying multiple selection objectives and providing graceful degradation when LLM services fail. We provide reproducible algorithms, design principles, and a foundation for future work on adaptive assessment---from technical interviews to educational assessment, certification, and skill evaluation.

\noindent \textbf{Take-home message.} Beyond one-size-fits-all, effective AI-assisted interviewing requires \emph{adaptive} difficulty, \emph{multi-priority} selection, and \emph{hybrid} evaluation with fallback. Our system demonstrates that these three pillars can be implemented reproducibly and that they work synergistically to improve both assessment validity and candidate experience.
