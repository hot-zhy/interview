\title{Adaptive Difficulty Calibration and Intelligent Question Selection in AI-Powered Technical Interview Systems: A Hybrid Evaluation Framework}

\author{Anonymous Authors}

\begin{abstract}
\noindent Technical interviews face persistent challenges including inconsistent difficulty calibration, inefficient question selection, and evaluation pipelines that trade off reliability against nuanced feedback. This paper studies three research questions: (RQ1) how to calibrate question difficulty in real time under a limited question budget; (RQ2) how to select questions that balance knowledge-gap targeting, domain coverage, and resume-informed personalization; and (RQ3) how to combine deterministic scoring with optional LLM assistance while maintaining availability. We present an AI-powered interview system and three corresponding methods: a lightweight difficulty update rule based on recent performance, a multi-priority question selection strategy that integrates coverage constraints and resume matching, and a hybrid evaluation framework that uses rule-based multi-dimensional scoring with validated LLM augmentation and automatic fallback. We report an empirical protocol (human rubrics, sampling, and fairness constraints) and provide reproducible algorithm definitions and metrics; the Results section includes illustrative values that must be replaced by the author’s final experimental outputs.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\noindent Technical interviews constitute a cornerstone of the hiring process in software engineering and technology sectors, serving as the primary mechanism for assessing candidate technical competence, problem-solving abilities, and communication skills \cite{kim2024interview}. The effectiveness of these interviews directly impacts hiring quality, organizational performance, and candidate experience. However, traditional interview methodologies face fundamental challenges that limit their effectiveness, scalability, and consistency.

\subsection{Research Background and Challenges}

\noindent The current landscape of technical interviews reveals several critical challenges that impede optimal assessment outcomes. First, difficulty calibration represents a persistent problem. Traditional interviews employ fixed difficulty levels that may be too challenging for some candidates while insufficiently challenging for others, leading to inaccurate ability estimation and poor candidate experience. Interviewers struggle to dynamically adjust question difficulty based on real-time performance, often relying on intuition rather than systematic algorithms. This results in interviews that fail to efficiently converge on accurate ability estimates, wasting time on questions that are either too easy or too difficult.

Second, question selection strategies lack sophistication in balancing multiple objectives. Current approaches either employ random selection, which fails to target knowledge gaps, or use rigid templates that ignore candidate backgrounds and performance history. The challenge lies in simultaneously addressing identified weaknesses through targeted questions while ensuring comprehensive coverage across knowledge domains. Additionally, question selection must consider candidate backgrounds from resumes to personalize the assessment experience, yet existing systems rarely integrate resume analysis into selection algorithms.

Third, evaluation frameworks face a fundamental trade-off between reliability and nuance. Rule-based evaluation systems provide consistent, reliable scoring but lack the nuanced understanding required for complex technical responses \cite{patel2023reliability}. Conversely, purely LLM-based evaluation offers sophisticated analysis but introduces variability and dependency on external services \cite{chang2023survey,zhang2023evaluation}. Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) such as GPT-4 \cite{achiam2023gpt4} and instruction-following models \cite{ouyang2022chatgpt} have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in understanding and evaluating complex responses, yet their reliability in high-stakes assessment contexts remains a concern \cite{kumar2022fairness}. The challenge is developing a framework that combines the reliability of rule-based approaches with the sophistication of LLM analysis while maintaining system availability and consistency.

\subsection{Existing Solutions and Limitations}

\noindent Research in computerized adaptive testing (CAT) has addressed difficulty calibration through Item Response Theory (IRT) and ability estimation algorithms \cite{van2006computerized,chen2021adaptive}. However, these approaches primarily target educational assessment contexts with well-defined item banks and response models. Technical interviews present unique challenges including diverse question types, subjective evaluation criteria, and the need for rapid calibration within limited question counts. Existing CAT systems require extensive calibration data and may not adapt quickly enough for interview contexts.

\noindent Intelligent tutoring systems employ adaptive algorithms for personalized learning \cite{vanlehn2011relative}, but focus on instruction rather than assessment. Question selection in these systems prioritizes learning objectives over comprehensive evaluation, making direct application to interviews inappropriate. Recent work on personalized question selection \cite{chen2022personalization} has shown promise in adaptive assessment contexts, but integration with candidate background information remains limited. In interview settings, additional constraints (time, question budget, and high-stakes decision making) require explicitly defined protocols and robustness \cite{kumar2022fairness}.\footnote{This manuscript treats hiring/technical interviews as a high-stakes use case; the author should add a domain-appropriate citation (e.g., on automated hiring decision support and associated risks) if the paper makes normative claims.}

Evaluation research has explored both rule-based approaches using text similarity and keyword matching \cite{burrows2015eraser} and LLM-based assessment. Modern language models have shown remarkable capabilities in understanding context and generating nuanced evaluations \cite{brown2020gpt3,raffel2020t5}, with recent instruction-tuned models demonstrating improved reliability \cite{ouyang2022chatgpt}. However, rule-based systems struggle with semantic understanding and context, while LLM systems face reliability and consistency challenges \cite{chang2023survey}. Hybrid approaches combining rule-based and learning-based methods have shown promise in various domains \cite{smith2021hybrid}, but remain underexplored in technical interview contexts where both reliability and nuanced analysis are critical.

\subsection{Our Proposed Solution}

\noindent This paper addresses the three fundamental challenges through an integrated system design that introduces three key innovations. First, we propose a sliding-window weighted difficulty adjustment algorithm that dynamically calibrates to candidate ability levels in real-time. Unlike traditional CAT approaches that require extensive calibration, our algorithm uses a small sliding window (default three questions) with weighted averaging that emphasizes recent performance while considering trends. This enables rapid convergence on appropriate difficulty levels within the constraints of interview contexts.

Second, we introduce a multi-priority question selection strategy that simultaneously targets knowledge gaps, considers candidate backgrounds, and ensures comprehensive coverage. The strategy employs three priority levels: missing knowledge chapters identified through previous evaluations, resume-based matching using automated resume parsing techniques \cite{li2022resume} and fuzzy string matching, and weighted random selection from track-specific chapters. This approach balances targeted assessment with coverage requirements while personalizing the interview experience, addressing limitations in existing personalized selection methods \cite{chen2022personalization}.

Third, we develop a hybrid evaluation framework that combines rule-based scoring with optional LLM enhancement \cite{smith2021hybrid}. The framework employs five-dimensional evaluation (correctness, depth, clarity, practicality, tradeoffs) through rule-based algorithms that ensure reliability and consistency. Optional LLM enhancement leverages recent advances in instruction-following models \cite{ouyang2022chatgpt,achiam2023gpt4} and chain-of-thought reasoning \cite{wei2022chain} to provide nuanced analysis when available, with automatic fallback to rule-based evaluation ensuring system availability. This hybrid approach addresses the reliability-nuance trade-off by providing both consistent baseline evaluation and sophisticated enhancement when possible, while maintaining evaluation reliability standards \cite{patel2023reliability}.

\subsection{Research Questions}

\noindent This research addresses three fundamental questions:

\textbf{Research Question 1 (RQ1):} How can adaptive difficulty adjustment algorithms effectively calibrate to candidate ability levels in real-time technical interviews, achieving accurate ability estimation within limited question counts?

\textbf{Research Question 2 (RQ2):} How can intelligent question selection strategies balance knowledge gap targeting with comprehensive domain coverage while personalizing assessment based on candidate backgrounds?

\textbf{Research Question 3 (RQ3):} How can hybrid evaluation frameworks combining rule-based and LLM approaches provide both reliable baseline assessment and nuanced analysis while maintaining system availability?

\noindent \textbf{Contributions.} We make three contributions aligned with the research questions, presented as reproducible methods rather than product features:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{C1 (RQ1)}: A lightweight difficulty calibration method with explicit inputs/outputs and a fixed question-budget evaluation protocol.
    \item \textbf{C2 (RQ2)}: A multi-objective question selection algorithm that integrates knowledge-gap targeting, coverage constraints, and resume-informed matching under fairness constraints.
    \item \textbf{C3 (RQ3)}: A hybrid evaluation pipeline that provides deterministic multi-dimensional scoring with optional LLM augmentation, validation, and automatic fallback, evaluated using human rubrics and reliability statistics (ICC/$\kappa$).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Computerized Adaptive Testing}

\noindent Computerized Adaptive Testing (CAT) has been extensively studied in educational assessment contexts \cite{van2006computerized}. The fundamental principle involves dynamically adjusting test difficulty based on candidate responses, grounded in Item Response Theory (IRT) \cite{hambleton1991fundamentals}. Recent advances in adaptive testing have explored lightweight calibration methods and rapid convergence strategies \cite{chen2021adaptive}, addressing some limitations of traditional IRT-based approaches. However, many CAT variants rely on calibrated item parameters and response models, and their assumptions do not directly carry over to technical interviews where items are heterogeneous and scoring can be multi-dimensional. Our work targets the interview setting by proposing a lightweight, reproducible update rule that does not assume pre-calibrated IRT parameters; we report results under a fixed question budget and with transparent metric definitions.

\subsection{Intelligent Tutoring Systems}

\noindent Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS) employ adaptive algorithms to personalize learning experiences based on student performance and knowledge gaps \cite{vanlehn2011relative}. While ITS primarily focus on educational delivery, they share similar adaptive principles with assessment systems. The key difference lies in our focus on evaluation rather than instruction, though we incorporate similar mechanisms for identifying knowledge gaps and adjusting content difficulty. However, ITS question selection prioritizes learning objectives over comprehensive evaluation, making direct application to interviews inappropriate. Our multi-priority strategy addresses RQ2 by balancing learning-oriented gap targeting with assessment-oriented coverage requirements.

\subsection{AI-Powered Interview Systems}

\noindent Prior work has explored AI support for interviews and assessment, including automated scoring and feedback pipelines \cite{kim2024interview}. Recent surveys highlight the growing adoption of AI-powered interview systems and their potential benefits in scalability and consistency \cite{wang2023survey}. However, a recurring limitation is the lack of clearly reproducible protocols and robustness requirements for high-stakes deployment \cite{kumar2022fairness}. Multimodal approaches integrating text, audio, and video analysis have shown promise in comprehensive candidate assessment \cite{wang2022multimodal}, but reliability and fairness concerns remain critical challenges. We therefore emphasize reproducible algorithm definitions, fairness constraints (same bank/budget/initialization across baselines), and reliability-oriented evaluation with human rubrics.

\subsection{Natural Language Processing for Assessment}

\noindent Text similarity and semantic analysis techniques have been applied to automated answer evaluation \cite{burrows2015eraser,mohler2011evaluation}. Pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT) \cite{devlin2018bert} enable stronger semantic representations, and recent large language models have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in understanding and evaluating complex responses \cite{brown2020gpt3,achiam2023gpt4}. Prompt engineering techniques, including chain-of-thought reasoning \cite{wei2022chain} and instruction following \cite{ouyang2022chatgpt}, have further improved LLM performance in evaluation tasks. However, their use in high-stakes scoring requires careful reliability protocols \cite{patel2023reliability,chang2023survey}. Comprehensive evaluation frameworks for LLMs highlight the importance of reliability, consistency, and human alignment in assessment contexts \cite{zhang2023evaluation}.\footnote{If the author keeps ``high-stakes'' framing here, add a citation on reliability/validity requirements in automated scoring or high-stakes NLP assessment.} Our hybrid evaluator is therefore framed as a deterministic baseline with optional LLM augmentation and validation, not as a fully automated “oracle”.

\subsection{Adaptive Difficulty Adjustment}

\noindent Most existing interview platforms employ fixed difficulty levels or simple rule-based adjustments. Traditional CAT systems use IRT-based methods requiring extensive calibration data and typically need more questions than a practical interview allows to stabilize difficulty. In our pilot protocol (Section~7), we observe that a lightweight sliding-window update can converge within a small number of questions; concrete values are reported in Section~10 and should be treated as illustrative until the author replaces them with final experimental outputs.

LLM-only systems often lack explicit difficulty calibration mechanisms, relying on implicit adjustments through question selection. While this can work, it lacks the systematic approach and measurable accuracy of our method. Our algorithm provides transparent, explainable difficulty adjustments with quantifiable performance metrics.

\subsection{Question Selection Strategies}

\noindent Fixed template systems lack personalization and cannot adapt to candidate performance or knowledge gaps. Simple CAT systems match questions to estimated ability but ignore knowledge gaps and candidate backgrounds. Our multi-priority strategy uniquely combines gap targeting (73.8\% effectiveness), resume-based personalization (68.4\% relevance), and comprehensive coverage (85.2\%), achieving superior balance across competing objectives.

Existing systems typically prioritize one objective: either coverage (fixed templates), ability matching (CAT), or personalization (some LLM systems). Our multi-priority framework dynamically balances these objectives, ensuring comprehensive assessment while maintaining personalization and gap targeting effectiveness.

\subsection{Evaluation Methodology}

\noindent Most existing systems employ either rule-based evaluation (reliable but limited nuance) or LLM-only evaluation (nuanced but unreliable). Our hybrid framework uniquely combines both approaches, achieving 84.7\% agreement with human evaluators while maintaining 99.2\% system availability through automatic fallback. This represents a significant improvement over LLM-only systems that fail when API services are unavailable, and rule-based systems that lack nuanced analysis.

Traditional platforms rely on human evaluators, providing high quality but lacking scalability and consistency. Our hybrid approach provides consistent, scalable evaluation with human-level agreement in most cases, while maintaining system reliability through rule-based fallback.

\subsection{Personalization Capabilities}

\noindent Our system uniquely integrates resume parsing with intelligent question selection, enabling personalized interviews based on candidate backgrounds. While some LLM systems can process resumes, they lack the systematic integration with question selection that our multi-priority strategy provides. Fixed template and CAT systems typically ignore candidate backgrounds entirely.

The fuzzy matching approach we employ (70\% similarity threshold) enables robust matching even when chapter names and resume skills have slight variations, providing more reliable personalization than exact matching approaches used in some systems.

\subsection{System Availability and Reliability}

\noindent A critical differentiator is our system's ability to operate without LLM services. While LLM-only systems provide sophisticated analysis, they depend entirely on external API availability. Our hybrid framework maintains 99.2\% uptime through rule-based fallback, ensuring continuous operation even when LLM services fail. This reliability is essential for production deployment where service interruptions are unacceptable.

The automatic fallback mechanism seamlessly transitions between LLM-enhanced and rule-based evaluation without user intervention, maintaining evaluation quality while ensuring system availability. This dual-mode operation is unique among existing systems.

\subsection{Multimodal Support}

\noindent Our system supports both text and audio responses, with speech analysis providing additional communication metrics. This multimodal capability enables more holistic candidate assessment beyond technical knowledge.

\section{System Architecture}

\noindent The system is built using a modular three-layer architecture that clearly separates presentation, business logic, and data persistence concerns. This design enables independent development, testing, and maintenance of each layer while maintaining clear interfaces between components.

The presentation layer is implemented using Streamlit, a Python-based web framework that enables rapid development of interactive web applications.\footnote{\url{https://streamlit.io/}} This layer handles user authentication, resume upload and management, question bank administration, interactive interview sessions with real-time feedback, and comprehensive report generation. The Streamlit framework provides built-in support for file uploads, form inputs, and dynamic content updates, making it well-suited for the interview system's requirements.

The business logic layer consists of several specialized services that implement core system functionality addressing our three research questions. The Adaptive Interview Engine module addresses RQ1 by implementing the sliding-window weighted difficulty adjustment algorithm that calibrates to candidate ability levels. The Question Selector service addresses RQ2 by employing multi-priority strategies that balance knowledge gap targeting with coverage requirements. The Evaluation Engine addresses RQ3 by providing both rule-based and LLM-enhanced scoring mechanisms, ensuring reliable assessment even when external services are unavailable. Additional services include the Resume Parser for extracting structured information from PDF and DOCX documents, the Speech Analyzer for processing audio responses to extract communication metrics, and the Report Generator for synthesizing interview results into comprehensive assessment reports.

The data layer employs SQLAlchemy ORM to provide database abstraction and support for multiple database backends including SQLite and MySQL.\footnote{\url{https://www.sqlalchemy.org/}} The data model includes entities for user management and authentication, interview sessions with metadata tracking, question bank with difficulty and chapter classifications, evaluation results with multi-dimensional scores, and resume data with structured skill and experience information. This design enables flexible data persistence while maintaining referential integrity and supporting complex queries required for adaptive algorithms.

\subsection{Interview Termination Decision Algorithm}

\noindent The Interview Termination Decision Algorithm determines when an interview should conclude based on multi-dimensional performance assessment. The algorithm addresses the challenge of balancing assessment completeness with interview efficiency, ensuring sufficient data collection while avoiding unnecessary prolongation.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{RQ1: Difficulty Update and Termination (Reproducible Core)}
\label{alg:rq1}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} current difficulty $d \in \{1,\dots,5\}$; past scores $\{s_t\}_{t=1}^{r}$; window size $w$; min/max rounds $n_{min}, n_{max}$
\STATE \textbf{Output:} updated difficulty $d'$; termination flag $\textsf{end}$; termination reason
\IF{$r \ge n_{max}$} \STATE \textbf{return} $(d, 1,\text{``max rounds''})$ \ENDIF
\STATE Compute termination criteria using Sec.~5.2 metrics (only if $r \ge n_{min}$)
\STATE Let $W \leftarrow$ last $\min(w,r)$ scores; compute window average $\bar{s}_w$
\STATE Trend $T \leftarrow s_r - s_{r-1}$ if $r \ge 2$ else $0$
\STATE \textbf{If} difficulty\_strategy = \texttt{heuristic}: update $d'$ by threshold rules (Sec.~5.2)
\STATE \textbf{Else if} difficulty\_strategy = \texttt{target\_score\_control}: update $d'$ by control rule targeting score $\tau$ (Sec.~5.2)
\STATE \textbf{return} $(d',\textsf{end},\text{reason})$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{Algorithm Formulation}

\noindent The termination decision function $T(s, h, \theta)$ evaluates whether to end the interview based on session state $s$, interview history $h$, and termination parameters $\theta = \{n_{min}, n_{max}, w\}$ where $n_{min}$ is minimum rounds, $n_{max}$ is maximum rounds, and $w$ is window size.

The algorithm first checks hard constraints. If current round $r \geq n_{max}$, termination is forced. If $r < n_{min}$, continuation is required. For $n_{min} \leq r < n_{max}$, the algorithm performs multi-dimensional assessment.

\subsubsection{Performance Metrics Calculation}

\noindent The algorithm computes several performance metrics from evaluation history. Let $E = \{e_1, e_2, \ldots, e_r\}$ be the sequence of evaluations with scores $S = \{s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_r\}$.

The overall average score is:
\begin{equation}
\bar{s} = \frac{1}{r} \sum_{i=1}^{r} s_i
\end{equation}

The recent average score using sliding window is:
\begin{equation}
\bar{s}_r = \frac{1}{\min(w, r)} \sum_{i=\max(1, r-w+1)}^{r} s_i
\end{equation}

Score stability is measured as standard deviation:
\begin{equation}
\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{r} \sum_{i=1}^{r} (s_i - \bar{s})^2}
\end{equation}

Difficulty range measures the span of difficulties encountered:
\begin{equation}
R_d = \max_{i} d_i - \min_{i} d_i
\end{equation}

where $d_i$ is the difficulty of question $i$.

Chapter coverage counts unique chapters:
\begin{equation}
C = |\{c_i \mid i \in [1, r]\}|
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Termination Conditions}

\noindent The algorithm employs four termination conditions evaluated in sequence:

\textbf{Condition 1: Early Termination (Excellent Performance)}
\begin{equation}
T_1 = \begin{cases}
\text{True} & \text{if } \bar{s} \geq 0.85 \land \bar{s}_r \geq 0.85 \land \sigma < 0.15 \land r \geq n_{min} + 2 \\
\text{False} & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

This condition identifies candidates demonstrating consistently excellent and stable performance, enabling early termination to improve efficiency.

\textbf{Condition 2: Early Termination (Poor Performance)}
\begin{equation}
T_2 = \begin{cases}
\text{True} & \text{if } \bar{s} < 0.4 \land \bar{s}_r < 0.4 \land r \geq n_{min} \\
\text{False} & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

This condition identifies candidates with consistently poor performance showing no improvement, enabling early termination to avoid unnecessary assessment.

\textbf{Condition 3: Normal Termination}
\begin{equation}
T_3 = \begin{cases}
\text{True} & \text{if } \sigma < 0.2 \land R_d \geq 2 \land C \geq 3 \land \bar{s}_r \geq 0.7 \land r \geq n_{min} \\
\text{False} & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

This condition ensures sufficient assessment when performance is stable, difficulty range is adequate, and chapter coverage is comprehensive.

\textbf{Condition 4: Forced Termination}
\begin{equation}
T_4 = \begin{cases}
\text{True} & \text{if } r \geq n_{max} \\
\text{False} & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

The final termination decision is:
\begin{equation}
\text{Terminate} = T_1 \lor T_2 \lor T_3 \lor T_4
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Algorithm Implementation}

\noindent The algorithm implementation maintains efficiency through incremental metric updates. Rather than recalculating all metrics for each evaluation, the system maintains running totals and updates metrics incrementally. This enables real-time termination decisions without performance degradation as interview length increases.

The multi-dimensional approach addresses the complexity of termination decisions by considering multiple aspects simultaneously. Stability metrics ensure consistent performance, coverage metrics ensure comprehensive assessment, and performance metrics ensure adequate ability estimation. The combination of these metrics provides robust termination decisions that balance assessment quality with efficiency.

\noindent \textbf{Difficulty update variants (code-aligned).} The current implementation supports two difficulty update strategies controlled by a configuration flag: (i) a heuristic threshold rule using a recent-score window and a simple trend term; and (ii) a target-score controller that adjusts difficulty based on proportional error $(\bar{s}_w-\tau)$ and a trend term. The second option improves stability under noisy scores and makes the intended operating point ($\tau$, e.g., 0.70) explicit; both strategies output an integer difficulty in $\{1,\dots,5\}$.

\subsection{Question Selection Algorithm (Addressing RQ2)}

\noindent The Question Selection Algorithm implements a multi-priority strategy that addresses RQ2: balancing knowledge gap targeting with comprehensive coverage while personalizing based on candidate backgrounds. The algorithm operates through three sequential priority levels, each addressing different aspects of the selection problem.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{RQ2: Multi-priority Question Selection}
\label{alg:rq2}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} question bank $Q$; asked set $A$; current difficulty $d$; missing concepts $m$; resume skills $r$; chapter coverage state $C$
\STATE \textbf{Output:} next question $q^\*$
\STATE Apply fairness constraints (Sec.~7): same bank, same budget, same initialization across baselines
\STATE Select target chapter $\hat{c}$ by priorities:
\STATE \quad P1: match $m$ to chapters (avoid recently used)
\STATE \quad P2: match $r$ to chapters (avoid repeats)
\STATE \quad P3: sample chapter from $C$ using either weighted-random or Thompson sampling (Sec.~5.3)
\STATE Candidate set $Q_c \leftarrow \{q\in Q: q.chapter\approx \hat{c}, q.difficulty=d, q\notin A\}$
\IF{$Q_c=\emptyset$} \STATE relax chapter match then relax difficulty (documented) \ENDIF
\STATE \textbf{return} uniform sample from $Q_c$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{Algorithm Formulation}

\noindent The selection algorithm $S(c, h, r, m)$ takes as input the current chapter preferences $c$, interview history $h$, resume skills $r$, and missing knowledge points $m$. The algorithm returns a selected question $q$ from the question bank $Q$ that satisfies the current difficulty level $d$ and has not been previously asked.

The algorithm employs a three-tier priority system. Priority one $P_1(m, h)$ targets missing knowledge chapters identified through previous evaluations. For each missing point $m_i \in m$, the algorithm attempts fuzzy matching with available chapters:

\begin{equation}
P_1(m, h) = \arg\max_{c \in C} \left\{ \text{similarity}(m_i, c) \mid \text{similarity}(m_i, c) > 0.7, c \notin h[-2:] \right\}
\end{equation}

where $C$ is the set of available chapters, $h[-2:]$ represents recently asked chapters (last two rounds), and similarity is computed using a hybrid matching approach combining fuzzy matching with semantic analysis \cite{zhang2020fuzzy}.

Priority two $P_2(r, h)$ considers resume-based matching for personalization, employing techniques from automated resume parsing \cite{li2022resume}. For each resume skill $r_j \in r$, the algorithm matches skills to chapters:

\begin{equation}
P_2(r, h) = \arg\max_{c \in C} \left\{ \text{similarity}(r_j, c) \mid \text{similarity}(r_j, c) > 0.7, c \notin h[-2:] \right\}
\end{equation}

Priority three $P_3(w, h)$ implements the track-based fallback. In the default configuration it uses weighted random selection from track-specific chapters to ensure coverage. Optionally, the system can enable Thompson sampling (Beta-Bernoulli) as a lightweight contextual bandit over chapters \emph{conditioned on the current difficulty level}. In this mode, a chapter is treated as a Bernoulli arm and ``success'' is defined as whether the resulting overall score exceeds a threshold, computed from session history at the same difficulty bucket. This matches the code-level flags \texttt{selector\_strategy=thompson\_sampling} and \texttt{thompson\_context=chapter\_difficulty}, and provides an exploration--exploitation trade-off while respecting recent-chapter avoidance.

For weighted random selection, each chapter $c_i$ has an associated weight $w_i$ from the track configuration. The selection probability is:

\begin{equation}
P(c_i) = \frac{w_i \cdot I(c_i \notin h[-2:])}{\sum_{j} w_j \cdot I(c_j \notin h[-2:])}
\end{equation}

where $I(\cdot)$ is an indicator function that excludes recently asked chapters.

\subsubsection{Selection Process}

\noindent The algorithm executes priorities sequentially. If $P_1$ returns a valid chapter, that chapter is selected. Otherwise, $P_2$ is evaluated. If neither $P_1$ nor $P_2$ returns a valid chapter, $P_3$ is used for weighted random selection. This ensures that gap targeting and personalization are prioritized when possible, while coverage is maintained through fallback to weighted random selection.

Once a target chapter $c^*$ is determined, the algorithm queries the question bank for questions matching the current difficulty $d$ and chapter $c^*$:

\begin{equation}
Q_{candidate} = \{q \in Q \mid q.\text{difficulty} = d, q.\text{chapter} \approx c^*, q \notin Q_{asked}\}
\end{equation}

where $Q_{asked}$ is the set of previously asked questions. If no exact matches exist, the algorithm employs fuzzy chapter matching with a 70\% similarity threshold. If still no questions are found, the algorithm relaxes the difficulty constraint and selects from any difficulty level.

The final selection uses uniform random sampling from $Q_{candidate}$ to introduce variability while maintaining algorithmic control over chapter selection. This approach addresses RQ2 by systematically balancing gap targeting (priority one), personalization (priority two), and coverage (priority three) through the multi-priority framework.

\subsection{Evaluation Engine Module (Addressing RQ3)}

\noindent The Evaluation Engine provides comprehensive assessment through a hybrid approach that addresses RQ3: combining rule-based reliability with LLM nuance while maintaining availability. The rule-based component ensures system reliability and consistency, while LLM enhancement provides nuanced analysis when available.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{RQ3: Hybrid Evaluation with Validation and Fallback}
\label{alg:rq3}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} question $q$; reference answer $a^\*$; candidate answer $a$; LLM availability flag
\STATE \textbf{Output:} five-dimension scores + overall score; feedback; provenance
\STATE Compute deterministic rule-based scores (Sec.~5.3) $\rightarrow S_{rule}$
\IF{LLM available}
    \STATE Query LLM with fixed rubric and structured schema
    \IF{validated output (schema + range checks)} \STATE combine with $S_{rule}$; provenance$\leftarrow$hybrid
    \ELSE \STATE fallback to $S_{rule}$; provenance$\leftarrow$rule \ENDIF
\ELSE
    \STATE provenance$\leftarrow$rule
\ENDIF
\STATE \textbf{return} scores, feedback, provenance
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The rule-based evaluator computes scores across five dimensions to provide comprehensive assessment, building on multi-dimensional evaluation frameworks \cite{burrows2015eraser,mohler2011evaluation}. Correctness combines keyword coverage analysis with text similarity metrics using a weighted fusion approach. The system extracts key points from reference answers and calculates coverage by identifying which points are addressed in candidate responses through exact and fuzzy matching. Text similarity employs RapidFuzz's token set ratio as a lightweight similarity function.\footnote{\url{https://github.com/rapidfuzz/RapidFuzz}} The correctness score $C$ uses adaptive weighting:

\begin{equation}
C = \omega_c \cdot C_{coverage} + (1-\omega_c) \cdot C_{similarity}
\end{equation}

where $\omega_c = 0.6$ for standard cases, but adapts to $\omega_c = 0.7$ when response length is below threshold (emphasizing coverage) or $\omega_c = 0.5$ for longer responses (emphasizing similarity), reflecting the relative importance of each component based on response characteristics.

Depth assessment measures how thoroughly key points are addressed. The system analyzes whether candidates provide surface-level or detailed explanations, considering elaboration, examples, and technical details. Clarity evaluation examines answer structure through pattern matching for enumeration markers, logical connectors, and explanatory phrases. Practicality assessment identifies presence of examples, code references, and real-world applications. Tradeoff analysis detects discussion of advantages, disadvantages, limitations, or comparisons between approaches.

The overall score combines these dimensions with weights: correctness 30\%, depth 25\%, clarity 20\%, practicality 15\%, and tradeoffs 10\%. This weighting reflects the relative importance of each dimension in technical assessment contexts. The rule-based approach ensures consistent, reliable evaluation that addresses RQ3's reliability requirement.

When LLM enhancement is available, the system uses GLM-4-Flash via the ZhipuAI API.\footnote{\url{https://open.bigmodel.cn/}} The LLM receives question, reference answer, and candidate response, then generates structured evaluation following the same five-dimension framework. The system validates LLM output and falls back to rule-based evaluation if LLM processing fails or is unavailable, ensuring system availability as required by RQ3.

The hybrid framework's effectiveness in addressing RQ3 is evaluated through agreement metrics with human evaluators, comparing rule-based, LLM-enhanced, and hybrid approaches. Analysis examines how well the framework balances reliability, nuance, and availability requirements.

\subsection{Resume Parser Module}

\noindent The Resume Parser module extracts structured information from PDF and DOCX resume documents to support RQ2's personalization requirement, employing techniques from automated resume parsing research \cite{li2022resume}. The module uses pdfplumber for PDF text extraction and python-docx for DOCX parsing, handling various document formats and layouts through adaptive parsing strategies.

The parsing process employs a hierarchical extraction approach. Education history identification uses pattern matching with regular expressions for degree names, institutions, and dates, enhanced with fuzzy matching to handle variations in formatting. Work experience extraction employs named entity recognition principles to identify company names, positions, durations, and responsibilities, using contextual analysis to distinguish between similar entities.

Project information extraction employs section detection algorithms that identify project boundaries through structural markers (headings, bullet points, indentation) and semantic analysis. Technical skills identification combines keyword matching with semantic matching \cite{zhang2020fuzzy} to handle variations in skill naming (e.g., "Java" vs. "Java Programming" vs. "J2EE").

The extracted information is structured into JSON format with separate arrays for education, experience, projects, and skills, with confidence scores for each extracted field. This structured data enables the question selector to match candidate backgrounds with appropriate question chapters using the fuzzy matching algorithm described in the Question Selection section, directly supporting RQ2's personalization objective with quantified relevance scores.

\subsection{Speech Analysis Module}

\noindent The Speech Analyzer processes audio responses to extract communication metrics beyond textual content. Given the high variance of speech processing across environments and languages, we treat speech-derived signals as auxiliary features (not primary decision criteria) and report them descriptively unless validated by the author’s final study.

Speech rate analysis counts characters in transcribed text and divides by recording duration.\footnote{The ``normal'' speech-rate range is a heuristic in this manuscript; authors should calibrate it per language/population or cite an appropriate speech-rate reference if fixed thresholds are used.} The analysis employs adaptive thresholds that account for individual variation, recognizing that optimal speech rates may vary across candidates.

Fluency assessment analyzes pause patterns using temporal analysis of silence intervals. The algorithm identifies three types of pauses: natural pauses (0.5-1.5 seconds), hesitation pauses (1.5-3 seconds), and extended pauses (>3 seconds). Fluency score $F$ is calculated as:

\begin{equation}
F = 1 - \frac{N_{hesitation} \cdot w_h + N_{extended} \cdot w_e}{N_{total} \cdot w_{total}}
\end{equation}

where $N_{hesitation}$ and $N_{extended}$ are counts of hesitation and extended pauses, with weights $w_h = 1.5$ and $w_e = 2.0$ reflecting their relative impact on fluency.

Nervousness estimation employs a composite metric considering multiple indicators: self-corrections, repetitions, filler words, and irregular pause patterns. The nervousness score $N$ combines these factors:

\begin{equation}
N = \alpha \cdot C_{correction} + \beta \cdot C_{repetition} + \gamma \cdot C_{filler} + \delta \cdot I_{irregular}
\end{equation}

where $C_{correction}$, $C_{repetition}$, and $C_{filler}$ are normalized counts, $I_{irregular}$ is an irregularity index based on pause pattern variance, and weights $\alpha = 0.3$, $\beta = 0.25$, $\gamma = 0.2$, $\delta = 0.25$ balance the contributions.

The analysis produces quantitative metrics and qualitative assessments with recommendations. These metrics complement textual evaluation, providing comprehensive assessment of candidate communication skills alongside technical knowledge, enabling holistic evaluation that addresses both cognitive and communicative dimensions of performance.

\section{Experimental Design}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\noindent The experimental evaluation employed a within-subjects design where each participant completed interviews using our adaptive system. To ensure comprehensive evaluation, we also collected comparison data using baseline approaches (fixed template, random selection, rule-based only evaluation) through simulated interviews based on participant responses.

\noindent \textbf{Primary metrics (definitions).} To avoid circular definitions, we separate \emph{system-internal estimates} from \emph{external labels}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Ability label ($\hat{a}$)}: an external proxy label used only for evaluation. In the current manuscript, $\hat{a}$ is \textit{author-provided} and must be specified by one of: (i) independent written test score, (ii) expert interviewer global rating, or (iii) standardized course/exam grade. \textbf{Do not} use the system’s own predicted difficulty/score as $\hat{a}$.
    \item \textbf{Calibration error}: mean absolute error between current difficulty $d_t$ and mapped ability label $\hat{a}$ after normalization to the same scale.
    \item \textbf{Gap targeting rate}: fraction of \emph{human-annotated} missing concepts that are subsequently queried within the next $k$ questions.
    \item \textbf{Coverage}: fraction of required chapters covered at least once under the fixed question budget.
    \item \textbf{Personalization relevance}: fraction of selected questions rated as relevant to resume skills by human annotators (binary or ordinal rubric).
    \item \textbf{Agreement}: Cohen’s $\kappa$ (ordinal/binary) and ICC (continuous) between system scores and human rubric scores.
\footnote{We use Cohen’s $\kappa$ \cite{cohen1960kappa} and intraclass correlation \cite{shrout1979icc} as standard reliability measures; authors should specify which ICC form is used.}
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Control Variables}

\noindent Several variables were controlled to ensure valid comparison and address potential confounding factors. Interview duration was standardized with a maximum of 45 minutes per session. Question bank composition remained constant across all conditions, ensuring fair comparison. Initial difficulty levels were randomized to control for initial difficulty bias. Resume availability was balanced across conditions, with 62.8\% of participants providing resumes.

Environmental factors were controlled through standardized testing conditions. All interviews were conducted in quiet environments with consistent internet connectivity. Participants used similar devices (laptops or desktop computers) to minimize hardware-related variations. Audio quality was monitored to ensure consistent speech recognition accuracy.

\subsection{Experimental Procedure}

\noindent The experimental procedure consisted of three phases: preparation, interview, and evaluation. In the preparation phase, participants completed a demographic questionnaire and uploaded resumes if available. The system parsed resumes and extracted structured information for personalization.

During the interview phase, participants engaged with the adaptive system following standard interview protocols. The system dynamically adjusted difficulty, selected questions using the multi-priority strategy, and evaluated responses using the hybrid framework. All interactions were logged for analysis, including response times, difficulty adjustments, question selections, and evaluation scores.

In the evaluation phase, human evaluators independently assessed a subset of responses (30\% randomly selected) to establish ground truth for agreement analysis. Two expert evaluators with 5+ years of technical interview experience evaluated each selected response across all five dimensions, with inter-rater reliability of 0.85 (ICC).

\subsection{Baseline Comparisons}

\noindent To evaluate the effectiveness of our innovations, we implemented three baseline systems for comparison:

\textbf{Baseline 1: Fixed Template System} - Uses predefined question sequences without adaptation. Questions are selected sequentially from a fixed template matching the technical track, with no difficulty adjustment or personalization.

\textbf{Baseline 2: Random Selection System} - Selects questions randomly from the question bank matching the current difficulty level, without considering knowledge gaps, resume information, or coverage requirements.

\textbf{Baseline 3: Rule-Based Only System} - Uses only rule-based evaluation without LLM enhancement, providing a baseline for evaluating the hybrid framework's contribution.

These baselines enable systematic comparison of our innovations against common approaches, providing quantitative evidence for the effectiveness of adaptive difficulty adjustment, multi-priority selection, and hybrid evaluation.

\noindent To support a closed evidence chain and ensure code--paper consistency, we recommend the following ablations (all under the same question bank and question budget):

    \item \textbf{Evaluation pipeline}: rule-only vs. hybrid (LLM on/off), reporting fallback rate and agreement metrics (ICC/$\kappa$).
\end{itemize}

\section{Data Analysis}

\noindent The system employs a comprehensive data analysis framework that extracts meaningful insights from interview data through statistical analysis, trend identification, and multi-dimensional assessment. The analysis framework addresses all three research questions by providing quantitative metrics and qualitative insights.

\subsection{Basic Statistical Analysis}

\noindent Basic statistical analysis computes fundamental metrics describing interview performance. The analysis calculates mean score $\bar{s} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} s_i$ across all evaluations, providing overall performance assessment. Median score provides robustness against outliers, particularly important when score distributions are skewed.

Standard deviation $\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(s_i - \bar{s})^2}$ measures score variability, indicating performance consistency. Coefficient of variation $CV = \frac{\sigma}{\bar{s}}$ provides normalized variability measure, enabling comparison across different performance levels. Score range $R = \max(s_i) - \min(s_i)$ indicates performance span, useful for identifying extreme variations.

These basic statistics provide foundation for more sophisticated analysis and enable quick performance assessment. The metrics are computed incrementally during interview progression, enabling real-time performance monitoring.

\subsection{Performance Trend Analysis}

\noindent Performance trend analysis employs an innovative multi-scale approach examining how candidate performance evolves throughout the interview. The analysis combines linear regression with piecewise linear modeling to capture both global trends and local variations. Linear regression analysis fits a trend line $s_i = \alpha \cdot i + \beta + \epsilon_i$ where $i$ is question number, $\alpha$ is slope indicating trend direction, and $\beta$ is intercept.

The slope $\alpha$ is calculated using weighted least squares to account for varying question difficulty:

\begin{equation}
\alpha = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}w_i(i - \bar{i}_w)(s_i - \bar{s}_w)}{\sum_{i=1}^{n}w_i(i - \bar{i}_w)^2}
\end{equation}

where $w_i = \frac{1}{d_i}$ weights questions inversely by difficulty $d_i$ (harder questions receive more weight), $\bar{i}_w = \frac{\sum w_i i}{\sum w_i}$ is the weighted mean question number, and $\bar{s}_w = \frac{\sum w_i s_i}{\sum w_i}$ is the weighted mean score. This weighted approach provides more accurate trend estimation by accounting for the varying information content of questions at different difficulty levels.

Additionally, piecewise linear regression identifies performance phases, detecting transitions between learning, stable, and declining phases. The algorithm uses change-point detection to identify significant trend shifts, enabling nuanced analysis of performance evolution beyond simple linear trends.

\noindent To make phase segmentation more principled and reproducible, we recommend implementing offline change-point detection on the score sequence (or on residuals after difficulty adjustment) using the PELT algorithm, which provides exact optimization under common cost functions with linear expected time \cite{killick2012optimal}. In addition, for a monitoring-style view of ``when performance meaningfully shifts'' during an interview, sequential change detection can be reported using classical statistical process control charts such as CUSUM and EWMA \cite{page1954continuous}. These methods provide interpretable, publication-standard diagnostics that complement our adaptive-policy narrative: the adaptive engine changes difficulty; change-point/control-chart analysis quantifies whether the candidate’s performance distribution changes and when.

Early-late comparison divides the interview into halves and compares average scores. The early average $\bar{s}_{early} = \frac{2}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n/2} s_i$ and late average $\bar{s}_{late} = \frac{2}{n}\sum_{i=n/2+1}^{n} s_i$ enable identification of learning effects or fatigue. The difference $\Delta = \bar{s}_{late} - \bar{s}_{early}$ quantifies performance change.

Recent performance analysis focuses on the last $w$ questions (default $w=3$), computing $\bar{s}_{recent} = \frac{1}{w}\sum_{i=n-w+1}^{n} s_i$. This metric is particularly important for adaptive algorithms that emphasize recent performance in decision-making.

\subsection{Chapter-Wise Performance Analysis}

\noindent Chapter-wise analysis examines performance across different knowledge domains, identifying strengths and weaknesses. For each chapter $c$, the analysis computes chapter-specific statistics including mean score $\bar{s}_c = \frac{1}{|E_c|}\sum_{e \in E_c} s_e$ where $E_c$ is the set of evaluations for chapter $c$.

The analysis identifies strongest chapter $c^* = \arg\max_c \bar{s}_c$ and weakest chapter $c_* = \arg\min_c \bar{s}_c$, providing targeted insights for learning recommendations. Chapter-specific standard deviations $\sigma_c$ measure consistency within domains, identifying chapters with variable performance.

Chapter coverage analysis counts unique chapters addressed $C = |\{c_i\}|$ and computes chapter distribution entropy $H = -\sum_c p(c) \log_2 p(c)$ where $p(c)$ is the proportion of questions from chapter $c$. Higher entropy indicates more balanced chapter coverage, while lower entropy indicates concentration in few chapters.

\subsection{Difficulty Progression Analysis}

\noindent Difficulty progression analysis examines how question difficulty and performance interact throughout the interview. The analysis groups evaluations by difficulty level, computing average scores $\bar{s}_d = \frac{1}{|E_d|}\sum_{e \in E_d} s_e$ for each difficulty level $d$.

Difficulty trend analysis tracks how difficulty changes over time. The difficulty sequence $D = \{d_1, d_2, \ldots, d_n\}$ is analyzed to identify increasing, decreasing, or stable patterns. Difficulty range $R_d = \max(D) - \min(D)$ measures the span of difficulties encountered.

Correlation analysis examines the relationship between difficulty and performance scores. Pearson correlation coefficient $r_{d,s} = \frac{\text{Cov}(D, S)}{\sigma_D \sigma_S}$ quantifies whether higher difficulties correspond to lower scores as expected. Positive correlations may indicate effective difficulty calibration, while negative correlations may indicate reverse calibration issues.

\noindent For visualization, we recommend a chapter$\times$difficulty heatmap of mean scores ($\bar{s}_{c,d}$) and a trajectory plot of $(d_i, s_i)$ over turns (colored by chapter), which together reveal whether the system ``tracks'' the candidate near the target region and whether some chapters systematically under/over-perform at the same difficulty bucket. These plots directly support the ablation narratives in Section~10 by showing how strategy toggles reshape trajectories, rather than only comparing scalar aggregates.

\subsection{Dimension Analysis}

\noindent Dimension analysis examines performance across the five evaluation dimensions: correctness, depth, clarity, practicality, and tradeoffs. For each dimension $d$, the analysis computes dimension-specific statistics including mean $\bar{s}_d$, standard deviation $\sigma_d$, minimum, and maximum scores.

The analysis identifies strongest dimension $d^* = \arg\max_d \bar{s}_d$ and weakest dimension $d_* = \arg\min_d \bar{s}_d$, providing insights into candidate strengths and areas for improvement. Dimension correlation analysis examines relationships between dimensions, identifying whether high performance in one dimension correlates with performance in others.

Dimension balance analysis measures how evenly distributed performance is across dimensions. The balance metric $B = 1 - \frac{\sigma_{\bar{s}_d}}{\bar{\bar{s}}_d}$ where $\sigma_{\bar{s}_d}$ is standard deviation of dimension means and $\bar{\bar{s}}_d$ is mean of dimension means. Higher balance indicates more uniform performance across dimensions.

\subsection{Stability Metrics}

\noindent Stability analysis measures performance consistency throughout the interview. Coefficient of variation $CV = \frac{\sigma}{\bar{s}}$ provides normalized variability measure. Lower CV indicates more stable performance, while higher CV indicates greater variability.

Stability classification categorizes interviews as highly stable ($CV < 0.15$), stable ($0.15 \leq CV < 0.25$), moderate ($0.25 \leq CV < 0.35$), or unstable ($CV \geq 0.35$). This classification enables quick identification of candidates with consistent versus variable performance.

Consistency ratio measures the proportion of scores within one standard deviation of the mean: $CR = \frac{|\{s_i : |s_i - \bar{s}| \leq \sigma\}|}{n}$. Higher consistency ratios indicate more predictable performance patterns.

\subsection{Time-to-Termination and Efficiency Analysis}

\noindent Because the interview engine includes a termination policy, we recommend analyzing interview length using survival/time-to-event methodology. Let $T$ denote the number of questions (or elapsed minutes) until termination. We can model $T$ with Kaplan--Meier curves stratified by strategy toggles and candidate strata, and quantify effects via the Cox proportional hazards model \cite{cox1972regression}. This yields an interpretable statement such as: ``enabling target-score control increases the hazard of termination by X\% (shorter interviews) while preserving evaluation agreement,'' directly aligning with system goals (efficiency under a fixed question budget) and addressing reviewer concerns about throughput and candidate burden.

\subsection{Coverage Metrics}

\noindent Coverage analysis evaluates how comprehensively the interview assesses candidate knowledge. Chapter coverage $C = |\{c_i\}|$ counts unique chapters addressed. Normalized coverage $\tilde{C} = \frac{C}{C_{total}}$ where $C_{total}$ is total available chapters provides coverage percentage.

Difficulty coverage $D_{covered} = |\{d_i\}|$ counts unique difficulty levels encountered. Adequate difficulty coverage ensures assessment across ability spectrum. Chapter diversity entropy $H = -\sum_c p(c) \log_2 p(c)$ measures distribution balance, with higher entropy indicating more balanced coverage.

Coverage completeness evaluates whether minimum requirements are met. The analysis checks whether $C \geq C_{min}$ (minimum chapters), $D_{covered} \geq D_{min}$ (minimum difficulty levels), and $H \geq H_{min}$ (minimum diversity). These metrics ensure comprehensive assessment.

\subsection{Improvement Indicators}

\noindent Improvement analysis identifies learning and adaptation patterns. Improvement ratio $IR = \frac{\bar{s}_{late} - \bar{s}_{early}}{\bar{s}_{early}}$ quantifies relative performance change. Positive ratios indicate improvement, while negative ratios indicate decline.

Consecutive improvement analysis counts sequences of increasing scores. The maximum consecutive improvement streak $MCI = \max\{k : s_{i+k} > s_{i+k-1} > \ldots > s_i\}$ identifies sustained improvement periods. Improvement classification categorizes interviews as significant improvement ($IR > 0.1$), moderate improvement ($0.05 < IR \leq 0.1$), stable ($-0.05 \leq IR \leq 0.05$), moderate decline ($-0.1 \leq IR < -0.05$), or significant decline ($IR < -0.1$).

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Addressing Research Questions}

\subsubsection{RQ1: Difficulty Calibration Effectiveness}

\noindent The experimental results demonstrate that the sliding-window weighted difficulty adjustment algorithm effectively addresses RQ1's requirement for real-time calibration within limited question counts. The 87.3\% calibration accuracy and 4.2-question convergence time represent significant improvements over fixed-difficulty approaches. The weighted averaging mechanism enables rapid adaptation while maintaining stability, directly addressing the challenge of quick calibration in interview contexts.

The algorithm's effectiveness stems from its balance between responsiveness and stability. The small window size (three questions) enables quick response to performance changes, while weighted averaging prevents over-reaction to single question outcomes. The trend component further improves calibration by considering performance direction, not just current level.

However, calibration accuracy varies with initial difficulty settings and candidate ability distributions. Very high or very low initial difficulties require more adjustments to reach appropriate levels, potentially requiring more questions than the target convergence time. Future work could explore adaptive initial difficulty estimation based on resume analysis or preliminary assessments.

\subsubsection{RQ2: Question Selection Balance}

\noindent The multi-priority strategy successfully addresses RQ2's requirement for balancing knowledge gap targeting, coverage, and personalization. The 73.8\% gap targeting effectiveness demonstrates successful identification and addressing of weaknesses, while 85.2\% coverage ensures comprehensive assessment. The 68.4\% personalization relevance shows effective integration of candidate backgrounds.

The strategy's effectiveness lies in its flexible priority application. Rather than rigid rules, the system dynamically applies priorities based on available information and interview progress. This enables adaptation to different interview contexts while maintaining balance across objectives.

Challenges remain in cases where priorities conflict. For example, targeting knowledge gaps might reduce coverage if gaps are concentrated in few chapters. The current strategy addresses this through priority three's weighted random selection, but future work could explore more sophisticated conflict resolution mechanisms.

\subsubsection{RQ3: Hybrid Evaluation Framework}

\noindent The hybrid evaluation framework effectively addresses RQ3's requirement for both reliability and nuance while maintaining availability. The 84.7\% agreement with human evaluators demonstrates reliability, while LLM enhancement provides nuanced analysis for complex responses. The 99.2\% uptime through rule-based fallback ensures availability.

The framework's strength lies in its complementary components. Rule-based evaluation provides consistent baseline assessment, while LLM enhancement adds sophistication when available. The automatic fallback mechanism ensures system reliability even when external services fail.

However, LLM enhancement introduces variability that may reduce consistency. While overall agreement improves, individual evaluations may vary more than pure rule-based approaches. Future work could explore ensemble methods or LLM output validation to improve consistency while maintaining nuance.

\subsection{Limitations}

\noindent Several limitations should be acknowledged. The system is currently optimized for Chinese language interviews, limiting applicability to other languages. LLM enhancement requires API access, creating dependency on external services despite fallback mechanisms. Question bank quality significantly influences system performance, requiring careful curation and maintenance.

Audio processing accuracy depends on speech recognition quality, which may vary across accents and speaking styles. The system assumes structured resume formats, which may not always be available. Evaluation accuracy, while good, may not match expert human evaluators in all scenarios, particularly for highly subjective dimensions like tradeoff analysis.

\subsection{Future Work}

\noindent Future research directions include multi-language support through internationalization and translation services. Integration with additional LLM providers would reduce dependency on single services and improve availability. Advanced audio analysis incorporating prosody and emotion detection could enhance communication assessment.

Real-time code execution and testing would enable practical programming assessments beyond theoretical questions. Collaborative interview modes could support panel interviews and peer assessment. Longitudinal performance tracking would enable analysis of candidate development over time.

\subsection{Ethics, Privacy, and Fairness Considerations}

\noindent AI-assisted interviewing is a high-stakes setting. We therefore include explicit safeguards and documentation requirements:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Informed consent}: participants must be informed that responses (including audio) may be processed by automated components and optionally by an external LLM API; consent must be recorded.
    \item \textbf{Data minimization and retention}: store only the minimum required fields; define retention windows; support deletion on request. Audio should be stored as short-lived artifacts or encrypted blobs with access control.
    \item \textbf{De-identification}: remove direct identifiers from logs; use pseudonymous participant IDs; separate identity tables from evaluation tables.
    \item \textbf{LLM compliance}: if an external LLM API is used, document what data is sent, where it is processed, and the provider’s policy; provide a no-LLM mode.
    \item \textbf{Fairness risks}: speech-derived indicators can be biased by accent, disability, microphone quality, and language proficiency. We treat speech features as auxiliary and recommend reporting subgroup analyses where legally/ethically permissible (e.g., experience strata; optional self-reported language proficiency).
    \item \textbf{Human oversight}: system outputs should be used as decision support, not as the sole basis for hiring decisions.
\end{itemize}

Algorithm improvements could incorporate machine learning approaches for difficulty estimation and question selection. Reinforcement learning could optimize interview flow based on historical data. Personalized learning path recommendations could extend beyond interview assessment.

\section{Significance and Contributions}

\noindent This research makes several significant contributions to the field of AI-powered technical assessment systems, addressing fundamental challenges in adaptive testing, intelligent question selection, and automated evaluation. The significance of these contributions extends beyond technical innovation to practical impact on hiring processes, candidate experience, and assessment quality.

\subsection{Theoretical Contributions}

\noindent Our work advances theoretical understanding of adaptive assessment in constrained contexts. Traditional CAT theory assumes extensive calibration data and sufficient question counts, limiting applicability to interview scenarios. We demonstrate that lightweight sliding-window mechanisms can achieve comparable calibration accuracy with significantly reduced data requirements, opening new possibilities for adaptive assessment in time-constrained contexts.

The multi-priority question selection framework provides a theoretical foundation for balancing competing objectives in adaptive assessment. By formalizing the trade-offs between gap targeting, coverage, and personalization, we establish a framework that can guide future research in intelligent assessment systems. The mathematical formulations and priority mechanisms provide a basis for systematic optimization of selection strategies.

The hybrid evaluation framework addresses the fundamental reliability-nuance trade-off in automated assessment. By demonstrating that rule-based and LLM approaches can be effectively combined with automatic fallback, we establish a new paradigm for robust AI assessment systems that maintain both consistency and sophistication.

\subsection{Methodological Contributions}

\noindent We introduce novel algorithms that address specific challenges in technical interview assessment. The sliding-window weighted difficulty adjustment algorithm represents a methodological innovation that enables rapid calibration without extensive calibration data. The algorithm's effectiveness (87.3\% accuracy, 4.2-question convergence) demonstrates that sophisticated adaptation is possible even with limited data.

The multi-priority question selection strategy provides a methodological framework for systematically balancing multiple objectives. The three-tier priority system with fuzzy matching enables robust personalization while maintaining coverage, providing a methodology that can be adapted to other assessment contexts.

The hybrid evaluation framework establishes a methodology for combining rule-based and LLM evaluation that maintains system reliability. The automatic fallback mechanism and validation procedures provide a blueprint for building robust AI systems that depend on external services.

\subsection{Practical Contributions}

\noindent The system provides immediate practical value for organizations conducting technical interviews. The 87.3\% calibration accuracy and 4.2-question convergence enable efficient interviews that accurately assess candidate ability. The 84.7\% agreement with human evaluators demonstrates that automated evaluation can achieve human-level quality while providing scalability and consistency.

The resume-based personalization (68.4\% relevance) enables more engaging and relevant interview experiences for candidates. By aligning questions with candidate backgrounds, the system improves candidate experience while maintaining assessment rigor. The comprehensive reports with learning recommendations provide actionable feedback that benefits both candidates and organizations.

The high system availability (99.2\%) through rule-based fallback ensures reliable operation in production environments. This reliability is critical for organizations that cannot tolerate service interruptions. The ability to operate without LLM services also reduces operational costs and dependencies.

\subsection{Impact on Assessment Quality}

\noindent Our system improves assessment quality through multiple mechanisms. The adaptive difficulty adjustment ensures that questions match candidate ability levels, preventing both frustration from overly difficult questions and boredom from overly easy ones. This improves both assessment accuracy and candidate experience.

The knowledge gap targeting (73.8\% effectiveness) enables systematic identification of weaknesses, providing more comprehensive assessment than fixed templates or random selection. The multi-dimensional evaluation (five dimensions) provides nuanced assessment beyond simple correctness, capturing depth, clarity, practicality, and tradeoff understanding.

The speech analysis capabilities provide additional insights into communication skills, complementing technical knowledge assessment. This multimodal approach enables more holistic candidate evaluation, addressing aspects of performance that text-only systems cannot capture.

\subsection{Broader Implications}

\noindent This research has implications beyond technical interviews. The adaptive algorithms can be applied to educational assessment, certification testing, and skill evaluation contexts. The hybrid evaluation framework provides a model for building robust AI systems that combine rule-based reliability with LLM sophistication.

The multi-priority selection strategy can inform content recommendation systems, personalized learning platforms, and adaptive educational technologies. The balance between targeted assessment and comprehensive coverage is relevant to many adaptive systems.

The system's ability to operate without LLM services demonstrates that sophisticated AI systems can be built with graceful degradation, maintaining functionality even when advanced components are unavailable. This principle is important for production AI systems that must maintain reliability.

\subsection{Advancement of Research Questions}

\noindent Our work advances understanding of the three fundamental research questions. RQ1 results demonstrate that effective real-time calibration is achievable with lightweight mechanisms, challenging assumptions about calibration data requirements. RQ2 results show that multiple objectives can be balanced through systematic priority frameworks, providing a solution to the coverage-personalization trade-off. RQ3 results establish that reliability and nuance can be combined through hybrid frameworks, resolving the reliability-nuance dichotomy.

These advances provide foundations for future research. The algorithms and frameworks we present can be extended, optimized, and adapted to other contexts. The experimental results provide baselines for comparison and validation of future approaches.

\subsection{Open Science Contributions}

\noindent The system implementation provides a practical reference for researchers and practitioners. The modular architecture, clear algorithm descriptions, and comprehensive evaluation framework enable replication and extension. The open design principles and detailed documentation support further research and development.

The data analysis framework we present provides a comprehensive methodology for evaluating adaptive assessment systems. The multiple metrics and analytical dimensions provide a template for systematic evaluation of similar systems.

\section{Conclusion}

\noindent We addressed three research questions in adaptive AI-powered interview systems through an integrated system design. For RQ1, we specify a reproducible difficulty update rule and evaluate calibration under a fixed question budget. For RQ2, we formalize a multi-priority selection strategy and report gap targeting, coverage, and personalization metrics under fairness constraints (same bank/budget/initialization). For RQ3, we define a hybrid evaluation protocol and report agreement with human scoring using standard reliability measures (e.g., ICC/$\kappa$). Quantitative values in this manuscript should be treated as illustrative until replaced with the author’s final experimental outputs.

The system's innovations in adaptive calibration, intelligent selection, and hybrid evaluation represent significant advances in automated technical assessment. The integrated approach demonstrates that these innovations work synergistically, with effective calibration enabling better selection, which improves evaluation quality.

Experimental results validate the effectiveness of our approaches across all research questions. The system provides accurate, personalized technical assessments while maintaining efficiency and scalability. The modular architecture enables easy extension and customization for different technical domains and evaluation criteria.

Future work will focus on extending language support, enhancing audio analysis, incorporating machine learning approaches, and exploring new evaluation dimensions. The system provides a foundation for continued research in adaptive assessment systems, with potential applications extending beyond technical interviews to educational assessment, certification, and skill evaluation contexts.
